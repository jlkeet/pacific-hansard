Let me begin, Mr Speaker, by extending a special Greeting to you, to all your staff members, Honourable Members in the House and our people who are listening in to our debate.
The Constitution Amendment Bill we have before the House is a result of the review undertaken by the recently established Commission of Inquiry. On Page 46 of their Report, it says that although there are 25 Members of Parliament, there are persons capable of discharging a Minister’s responsibilities outside the circle of the 25 elected Members. The Report is not belittling our position in this House. However, they are saying that we are not the only ones who can carry out these responsibilities in a way, that they are correct because there are people in our communities who are knowledgeable in the work of a Minister but are reluctant to engage in politics and accordingly be elected. The Report also says that some of these people may also have

1455
the makings of a Minister, but they may not be successful at the polls. The Report referred to the system in the USA where the President selects the Cabinet. They are not chosen from the Congress or Senate but they are chosen from outside of these two bodies. In the majority of the Parliaments, who adopted the Westminster system, Ministers are selected from amongst the elected Members. In neighbouring Fiji, the power of the Prime Minister to select two Ministers from outside of Parliament was enshrined in its new Constitution. They are first appointed to the Senate and they are then made Ministers.
The Commission of Inquiry into our political system felt that while there are disadvantages, the pros outweigh the cons and as such one or two Ministers should be appointed by the Prime Minister from outside Parliament. They recommended that a total of seven Ministers form the Cabinet, with one of those to be appointed from outside of Parliament. And this particular Minister is eligible to speak in Parliament but not to vote. We have discussed this matter at length in our Caucus. The view of the Caucus was that we should leave this matter and decide upon it at some later date, either during the elections or thereafter. I reviewed this matter carefully and shared my views with Cabinet. There was an aspect of this recommendation which created a lot of concern and that was, “one Member of the seven-Member cabinet be elected from outside of Parliament”. Mr Speaker, this recommendation is negating the discretion of the Prime Minister to first choose within Parliament before looking outside it. That is why Cabinet decided to modify this recommendation to read, “That the discretion be given to the Prime Minister to first consider the Members of Parliament before considering candidates from outside of Parliament”. It is our reckoning that this broadens the scope of the Prime Minister to choose the right person for Minister, it does not restrict him to choose from outside only. This means that, if there is no suitable person from within Parliament then he shall look outside.
The main aim of this recommendation is to reduce the number of Members of Parliament. We have agreed that the number of Cabinet be reduced to seven, that is the first thing. Secondly, we have agreed that the Prime Minister has the discretionary right to look outside of Parliament for a Minister, if the need arises. The Prime Minister is not bound by law to confine himself to selecting one Minister from outside of Parliament. If he finds that he is able to meet all his requirements from within Parliament, then that is okay. Thirdly, as stated in the Bill, we have decided that the Act shall come into force after the next Election.
In conclusion, Mr Speaker, the main aim of this Constitution Amendment before us is to reduce the number of Members of Cabinet to seven, which complies with the number put forward by the Commission. Secondly, it gives the right to select a Minister from outside of Parliament. Thirdly, it means the Prime Minister is not bound to make his selection of one Minister, to outside candidates only. And fourthly, that this Act will come into effect next year.
Thank you, Mr Speaker.
