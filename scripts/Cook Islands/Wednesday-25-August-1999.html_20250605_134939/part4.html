HON. P. POKINO: Thank you Mr Speaker. Greetings to you Mr Speaker and to all your staff members.
First of all I would like to congratulate you Mr Speaker for attaining the seat of the Speaker of Parliament. Greetings to all Honourable Members in the House and also to our people who are listening to our debate this afternoon.
Mr Speaker, I move:
That the 1999/2000 Appropriation Bill be now Read a Second time
Mr Speaker, the Appropriation Bill marks a milestone for the restructuring program that this Government has implemented over the past three years.
I am delighted, Mr Speaker, to be able to announce a surplus of approximately $2.5 million from operations for the 1998/99 year. Not only this, Mr Speaker, but I am also able to announce that during the 1999/2000 period, the Government will spend over $3.2 million on infrastructure and assets funded from our own resources.
An impossible dream only three years ago and showing the first benefits from the hard decisions we were forced to take in 1996.
The improvement in the economy over this short period is a credit to all those both in the public and private sector who have worked to make the reform a reality. I am proud to be part of this Government that had the courage and perseverance to drive through the necessary reforms even though many of the actions taken were unpalatable to us.
Our achievements have not been without cost. It was heartbreaking for us to have to take the necessary steps to restore our economy to a sound basis in the knowledge that it would lead to the suffering of many of our people. It is unfortunate that our success in restoring the economy to a sounder basis has not immediately been reflected in an improvement in the daily lives of our people. However, what is certain is that if we had not taken the difficult steps that we did, then life for most people today would be far worse than it is and we would be facing a grim future.
Mr Speaker, Government is sensitive to the position of our people. It realises that the success of the reforms has been at a significant cost to our people. This budget, therefore, does not use the improvement in our position solely for the repayment of debt although this must remain an important part of our planning for the foreseeable future. This budget provides tangible evidence that the economy has improved and that the benefits are shared by all. There are in this budget significant increases for

social equity expenditures in line with our “fivepoint plan for economic growth” that set out our goals for recovery in 1996.
This Budget, Mr Speaker, is based on Crown Revenues from all sources except overseas aid of $48.6 million compared to the budget of $43 million and the actual revenue of $44.8 million for 1998/99. The main increases being import levies, company and income tax.
This Appropriation Bill has an increase of $10.8 million in expenditure to $56.8 million. Of this increase $4 million is for expenditure relating to Asian Development Bank loans for Education and Solid Waste Management projects that in previous years were treated inappropriately as Aid. $1 million is for capital expenditure that Ministries previously used depreciation to fund. This is the first budget to separately show capital expenditure in the Appropriation Bill.
While there are many variances the real increase in expenditure is $7.4 million of which $3.2 million is for major infrastructural assets and $4.2 million for increased Ministry expenses. This increase is partly offset by the use of $1.7 million of the 1998/99 surplus for the major capital expenditure.
There are ongoing increases in expenditure for Education, Health, Welfare, Cultural Development and Environmental Protection. In addition, there is a major increase in expenditure for infrastructure, assets so essential if Tourism is to remain our major sources of revenue.
Tourism is essential for the ongoing growth of our economy and in recognition of this, the vote for Tourism has been increased by $500,000 – a 25% increase on the amount allocated in 1998/99. While we would like to increase the expenditure on Tourism by an even greater amount we believe that this increase will have a significant influence and will build on the increased tourism numbers achieved during the last ten months.
There is planned to be a review of Education and Health during the coming year. However, we are not waiting until these reviews have been completed. In recognition of the importance of these two areas increases of 16% and 13% respective have been provided for in the budget.
The government is very appreciative of the Asian Development Bank for the loan that provided additional resources for Education. This budget provides funds to maintain the level of learning resources that the loan was able to provide. I am also pleased to announce that phase two of the new teachers salary scale will be implemented during 1999/2000 as promised.
More trainee nurses and specialist personnel are provided for in the Health budget. More importantly in the area of pharmaceutical supplies, laboratory stores and consumables Government has provided a significant increase so that the problems encountered in 1998/99, particularly in the outer islands, will not be repeated in 1999/2000.
Expenditure on the Environment in 1998/99 increased to $443,000 and has been further increased to $516,000 in this budget. Priority has been placed on the revision

and design of a national environmental framework, strengthening the Environmental Impact Assessment strategies; Education and Awareness, and most importantly a more efficient and effective management of our environment.
For many years maintenance of Government Buildings has tended to be ignored. In this budget the first steps have been taken to implement an ongoing maintenance and basic maintenance of Government buildings. In future years a system where Ministries will be required to pay market rents for the premises they occupy will be developed so that the buildings can be properly maintained and so that Ministries will better appreciate the value of the assets they use.
The Government recognises that the Airport Authority requires additional funds to maintain the assets and safety measures so necessary for the airport. In this budget the proportion of the Departure Tax paid to the Airport Authority has been increased from $200,000 to $300,000. Further increases will be made as the economy improves. A first class airport and services are essential for the maintenance and growth of Tourism.
At the present time the utility companies provide services for the country such as fire services and street lighting. These are reflected in a lower return from the utility companies and the provision of inadequate reserves for the replacement of assets when they come to the end of their useful life. Provision for the payment of social costs is shown on the Statement of Fiscal Responsibility but no funds have been allocated in this budget. Work is still being carried out to determine the appropriate cost for these services with the intention of making provision for them in future budgets so that Government assets can be properly maintained.
This budget, Mr Speaker, with its emphasis on social equity expenditure and infrastructural assets demonstrates the success of the reforms and provides evidence that the difficult decisions this Government had the courage to make are leading to a better economy and an improved standard of living for our people. We acknowledge with appreciation the very real contribution that the public and private sector and all of our people have made to achieve this success.
This Government, Mr Speaker, has not lost sight of the need to reduce debt. It is committed to meeting all our debt commitments particularly our commitments resulting from the Manila debt restructuring. This budget provides for all our debt requirements during 1999/2000 to be met and in addition for the repayment of the mortgages on the New Zealand properties. We will not lose sight of the need to reduce our heaving debt burden and as the economy continues to grow and resources become available further provision will be made for additional debt repayment.
This Government remains committed to continuing the reform process and the tight financial management of the past three years but just as resolutely we remain committed to investing in the Security, Health and Education of our people as resources permit.
Reform is a continuing process. We must continually adapt our policies and systems in an evolutionary way to changing circumstances and to reduce the need for crisis management. In other words action should be taken before a crisis happens and not
