The Honourable Minister for Education.
HON. N. PUNA: Thank you, Mr Speaker. I move:
That the Ministry of Education Annual Report for the period 1 July 1996 - 30 June 1997 be received
Before I speak on this Report, I would like to send my Greetings to my electorate in Aitutaki. I heard that you had an enjoyable Queen’s Birthday on Monday with all the sporting events that were staged on that particular day - the fishing competition, the vaka racing competition and also the games competition that was held at Araura College by the village of Reureu.
Now, I would like to send out congratulations to all the successful Mayors and Island Councillors at the recent Outer Islands Local Government Elections. In particular, the Island Council Elections on Aitutaki, to the successful candidate for the Mayor of the Island, Tai Herman, congratulations to you. And the three successful Councillors for Arutanga, Mr William Hewett, for Reureu Mr Tunui Mati and for Nikaupara, I believe the candidate declared the winner on election night, Mr Putangi Mose, has now lost the seat to Mr John Tearetoa on a recount. I believe that you have all accepted the results of these elections as an indication of the wish of the majority of the island and also the majority of the people of each village. I also believe that the Ministry of Outer Islands Development is waiting upon the resolving of the Pukapuka petition before declaring the final results for the Outer Islands Local Government Elections.
The upcoming three years will be a very important period in the history of our individual islands and also the country as a whole. The policy of Government declares that the Central Government considers the Local Governments of each Outer Island to be in a position to take on more responsibilities hence determining the destiny of their individual islands. I am very confident that you who have been elected to the positions of Mayors and Councillors will rise to the challenge. I also would like to send out my Greetings to all the Central Government Representatives of each Outer Island. You have a very important task to perform in representing the Central Government of each island. Together with the Chief Executive Officers in the Outer Islands, my prayer is that you will all paddle together in making progress for your islands.
To you, Mr Speaker, Sir, congratulations on the award that was accorded to you on the Queen's birthday. And, to the Honourable Iaveta Short and Ruta Short I extend my congratulations from my wife and family for your award of the most excellent Order of the British Empire, OBE. With other Members who have spoken before me, I

This report before the House, Mr Speaker, is just as important as the Bills that are brought in here. This is an opportunity for all the Members of this House to comment on the progress or non-progress of every Ministry of Government and every activity of Government that is brought into this House. I trust that every Honourable Member has done the Ministry of Education justice, by studying diligently the activities that have been undertaken by this Ministry for the period of 1st July 1996 to 30th June last year. This is the period before the transferring of education in the Outer Islands on the 1st July 1997 to this year, to the 30th June 1998, over to the Ministry of Outer Islands Development, MOID.
First of all, I would like to thank the Secretary of Education, Mr Eric Ponia. The Deputy Secretary of Education, Mr Lionel Brown and your senior management team, the staff of the Ministry of Education and all the teachers, all the school committees, the parents and pupils of the schools in the Cook Islands. I am very proud of your achievements for the period being reported upon. The only shortcoming in our operation within the Ministry of Education is that we do not tell the world or the country enough of what we are doing in our schools, in our colleges, pre-schools and in the Ministry itself. During this year, there was concentrated efforts on reviewing and improving the curriculum in our Primary and Secondary schools. And in our Secondary schools, there has been active participation by our friends from the New Zealand Qualifications Authority and consultants who were sent to us to help our principals, staff and committees to prepare themselves for the NZQA examinations. This Government made the decision three or four years ago that we were going to go with the NZQA examinations all the way. In the 1950's, Mr Speaker, you're a former teacher so you'd know what happened during those years, our schools here, at Tereora College, for the first time sat the New Zealand School Certificate Examinations. I believe that the first NZSC graduate was Mr Tony Utanga. As we were able to build up the Secondary level of education here in the Cook Islands we brought in the Sixth Form Certificate. But then, for some unknown reason, the Government of the day, I forgot the year, brought in a system of examination which was known as the USP Foundation examinations. Thank you.

HON N. PUNA: Mr Speaker, I would like to thank my colleagues, particularly the Members of the Opposition for granting me this extension. I appreciate it very much. Particularly the Leader of the Opposition, thank you for the courtesy extended to me. As I was saying, it was four years ago that Government made the decision to bring the seventh form examination entirely over to the NZQA examination, that being the Bursary examinations. That is why I said that Government decided to go with NZQA all the way. Because it is the logical step to take. And I want to say the record set by our students in form seven at Tereora College, we can all be proud of their record. They have really performed magnificently. For the last two years we have turned our attention to the majority of our children who were leaving our colleges without any formal qualifications in technical areas. And during this year under review that was the final spurt by the principals, and here I acknowledge the work done by the former principal of Mangaia College, the Honourable Jim Marurai, and all those principals out there for your hard work, it has paid off. At the beginning of this year we have had five of our colleges accredited, offering this credit system to our children.
I cannot sit down without mentioning the magnificent effort of our Outer Islands schools in the National Grade Six Examination. And I talk about the Mitiaro grade six level which provided the student who topped the national examination for 1997. And equal with Mitiaro Primary School was Avarua School and I want to congratulate the Avarua School for remaining at the top for the last three years. For three of the last four years, Avarua School has provided the top student for the country. Congratulations to you Avarua School. And on the sports field I would like to congratulate those schools who did well and I am referring to Avatea School who has been unbeaten in Athletics for so long. So, Mr Speaker, our job as teachers is to look at every individual pupil and determine what talents that pupil has, whether it be academic, singing, sports, and then to build on those talents of our children. That is the way we are running the Ministry of Education's program in our schools.
Mr Speaker, there are so many other people that I should mention in my short time speaking to this Report by the Ministry of Education. Let me mention one particular program which is called Te Ara Anga - the awakening. This is an excellent program by the Ministry of Education. This is looked upon with interest by other countries who are wanting to know more. It is an excellent program for the promotion of language development, in our language, English or whatever language you wish to learn.
Do not be deceived by the acronym of the Ministry of Education "M O E". Do not be deceived by it, Mr Speaker. This Ministry, if you read the Report properly, will tell you we are fully awake.
Before I sit down let me send best wishes to the conference by the Democratic Alliance Party today and over the next two days. Enjoy yourselves. Thank you.
