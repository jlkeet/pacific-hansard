As the representative of Her Gracious Majesty, Queen Elizabeth II, I welcome you to the forty-second Ceremonial Opening of the Parliament of the Cook Islands.
We are here to open the 42nd Session of the Parliament of the Cook Islands. Future historians will remember the 41st Parliament as one of our greatest, for never in the previous 31 years since independence had there been such a challenge and, yet, they met it with landmark legislation and with the never-to-be forgotten First Reform Budget.
With the support of many segments of the community, the 41st Parliament right-sized my Government, reformed our ways of financial management, restructured how the Public Service is employed, and made service the first goal of every Ministry. Even then, they did not rest but, with the PERCA Act, installed a watchdog committee to assure that what they had formed, no one could put asunder.
All that was achieved in just 140 stormy days. Then, in the waning months of 1996, when the shock, challenge and excitement of rescue and reform had subsided, there followed the week to week task of holding course, repairing the vessel and simply making headway against so many forces.
Those were the early days of privatisation when liquor was deregulated, the Akitua was sold and other assets including CITV, Cook Islands Telecom shares, and three Government-owned resorts were advertised for sale, absolute proof that my Government intended thereafter to govern, not compete.

Then came the National Retreat of January 1997, not a crisis-driven gathering, but one that chose to look ahead in a wholly constructive manner. Over four days the reform progress was reviewed. Each Ministry and most public concerns were aired, debated and decided.
From these, there emerged explicit financial principles which were brought together in a second historic document, the Budget Policy Statement: Te Akataka Anga Moni Ate Kavamani O te Kuki Airani Mataiti 1997/98. In it, my Government stated its intentions with respect to the collection and expenditure of public funds:
Comply with spending levels reflecting a far more focused role; Comply with publicly disclosed taxation and expenditure policies; Maintain a balanced budget with a contingency element for unforeseen
events;
Impose debt management disciplines; and Allocate funds via output-based criteria determined by national policy.
Not long after the National Retreat, my Government invited representatives of the leading institutions, non-Government agencies and productive sectors to represent their constituents in a National Development Council. The group came together willingly. Under the Chairmanship of the Deputy Prime Minister, NDC’s first major task at Cabinet’s request was to comment on the Second Reform Budget which Cabinet then finalised and which the 41st Parliament recently passed.
My Government believes that the Second Reform Budget is, if anything, more important than the first. That is because the first was accomplished under duress whereas the second evolved within an organised process managed by a cross section of the community, diverse groups all committed to continuing the reform and getting on to new recovery and growth. It was simply business as usual, the second of its kind and, presumably, the second of dozens more yet follow.
It was at the January retreat when my Government offered a vision towards which we will travel throughout the first decade of reform. Vision 2005 is simple but inclusive. As a nation, it states, “we are capable of achieving by the year 2005 a standard of living equal to if not better than any in the region, and in harmony with our culture and environment.”
My Government believes that Vision 2005 has taken hold. As a result of our combined pursuit of Vision 2005, my Government will:
Be self-reliant and not dependent on external aid; Encourage and enjoy private sector growth; Maintain that growth in harmony with the culture and environment that we
cherish;
Develop a substantial reserve fund with its surplus invested in health,
education, culture, sports and the preservation of our ecosystem; and
Operate with simple, transparent development policies but continuing to
raise national standards and ideals.

The Budget Policy Statement named three cornerstones necessary to successfully achieving these:
A resonant and vibrant culture, not isolated from the international
community;
Sustainable use of our natural resources; and Maintaining a practical approach to economic growth while setting
benchmark standards within the region.
My Government believes that the principles, guidelines and cornerstones which emerged from the reform process are beginning to yield the hoped for progress. A recent report by an Asian Development Bank economist stated:
“The projections for the next three years up to 2000 assume a resumption of the longer term growth trend with about 3% real growth in 1998 and 6% real growth in the two succeeding years. These trends are underpinned by stronger tourist expenditures and a resumption of investment activity -- mainly on tourist related facilities.
At about NZ$8,000 per capita, the Cook Islands has the highest GDP in the Pacific, but this is not evenly spread. Average incomes on Rarotonga are much higher than average incomes in the Outer Islands. A more equitable distribution of income among islands remains one of the Government’s major goals.”
In that last sentence, the economist put his finger squarely on what my Government believes is the major task that we as a nation must address. It is the equitable development of all the Outer Islands.
While it is conceivable that a right-sized central Government and a rejuvenated and growing private sector on Rarotonga can take us far towards Vision 2005, my Government believes that it is inconceivable that financial independence is wholly obtainable without greater private sector growth in the Outer Islands.
Nor will such growth occur without bold action. Towards this end, my Government has initiated a policy of devolution of much of central Government’s decision-making authority to the local Governments of the Outer Islands.
With only nine more years remaining to 2005, my Government decided to put its faith in the other half of its citizens as well. It believes there can be no better persons to manage day to day affairs on Pukapuka, Manihiki, or Mauke than the Pukapukans, Manihikians or Maukeans.
Nor is devolution confined to the Outer Islands, for my Government views the principle of decentralisation equally applicable to Rarotonga. This year, it will return many of its powers to the hands of three Vaka Councils, which together represent the traditional centers of this island’s local Government.

With regard to the coming year, there remain many challenges. My Government believes this is no time to relax or to bask in our successes. This is true of the community, of the Ministries, of the National Development Council and Cabinet and, most certainly, of you the 42nd Parliament.
The major legislative tasks for this session will include:
Amendments to bring other Acts into line with the new Ministry if Finance and Economic Development Act, with the Public Service Commission Act, with the Public Expenditure Review Committee and Audit Act.
Empowering legislation that will formalise the process of devolution. Legislation which expands the offshore industry and improves the
regulatory environment which governs it
Commercial legislation which will provide for consumer protection and fair
competition; and
Provision for the reform of tariffs and price controls.
Beyond the matter of legislation, much remains. My Government will give its highest priorities to:
Restructuring its debt; Facilitating growth in tourism, marine resources, agriculture - most
especially in the Outer Islands; and
Rationalising its aid policy.
NZODA, AUSAID and the Asian Development Bank are by far the largest donor agencies in support of our highest priority needs. Of the $10.0 million projected for aid in 1997/98, these three have committed to not less than $9.0 million of it. The remainder is to be provided by the World Health Organisation, United Nations agencies, the Forum Secretariat, Forum Fisheries Agency, France, Germany, and Canada - in that order.
With regard to NZODA and AUSAID, my Government has proposed that their assistance never again be used for budgetary support and, further, that all aid be focused on a few chosen and planned areas. Its reasoning for this is that, if we are committed to Vision 2005, the Cook Islands will achieve a standard of living that makes aid of any kind largely complementary.
My Government has made clear that, while not wanting budgetary aid, it welcomes project aid, but only for those projects that will encourage sustainability after 2005. In particular, these are:
Human resource building, especially in health and education; Infrastructure improvements, especially in waste management, coastal
protection and water systems; and
Outer Islands development, especially by means of project funding in
response to bids from the Outer Islanders themselves.

My Government hopes that Rarotonga’s private sector will seek ways to expand their interests into the Outer Islands, both through direct developments and in joint venture with the Outer Islands. My Government notes that one entrepreneur has boldly paved the way with much improved inter-island shipping and urges that others in their own way follow his example.
My Government believes that Outer Islands development is less a challenge than a major opportunity. A localised fishing fleet based in Penrhyn could harvest and sell 3,000 tonnes of fish per year on a sustainable basis, therein offering a $30 million dollar industry. With the extension of black pearl farming beyond Manihiki and Penrhyn to, for example, Pukapuka and Suwarrow, another $30 million addition to the nation’s GDP is foreseeable.
The Southern Group has similar opportunities, but with quite different resources. My Government intends to make every effort to facilitate an increase in visitor accommodations and activities here, first with significant new construction on Aitutaki and, then, over several years to each of the other islands in the Southern Group. These steps will lead inevitably to more commercial farming, both to service the spreading visitor industry and, then, through more export of agricultural products to Rarotonga and overseas.
Going beyond the Outer Islands to the nation as a whole, my Government wishes to facilitate foreign investment. The creation of a Development investment Board is one step it has taken in this direction. Changes to offshore legislation is another.
I hope that the Heads of Ministries will forgive me for leaving out the traditional litany of all the fine work that they have done and will do. My Government and the community are aware of the much-improved services you are providing with diminished funding and we are grateful for these.
As for the year 2005, I myself will be over 90 years young. The good Lord willing, I look forward to seeing our national vision become a fact and will, along with Lady Short, enjoy fruits of achieving that vision for many years thereafter. Having lived eight decades, I can assure you that each one, taken alone, seems a short period of time. Thus, nine years from now seems no time at all.
I hereby declare this 42nd Session of the Cook Islands Parliament open.
