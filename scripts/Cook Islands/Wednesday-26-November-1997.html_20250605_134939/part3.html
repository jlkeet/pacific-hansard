Value Added Tax Amendment Bill. Interrupted debate on the Second Reading.
I now call upon the Honourable Minister and Assistant Minister of Finance, Papamama Pokino, to reply and close the debate on the Second Reading.
HON. P. POKINO: Thank you Mr Speaker. Kia Orana to you, Kia Orana also to your staff and to Honourable Members of the House. Kia Orana also to our people listening in to our discussion. I will not be too long, Mr Speaker. I just want to reply to some of the questions that were raised yesterday.
Firstly, the question by the Honourable Member from Manihiki on the building materials that will be imported to rebuild some buildings on the island of Manihiki. I have been advised, Mr Speaker, that all those commodities, as a result of any disaster, are VAT exempted. That will be located in Clause 5 (d) of the principal Act.
The second question was raised by Mama Mau. I want to advise her yes, she can get a refund for the VAT they paid on their construction materials. As I said yesterday, Mr Speaker, that all these amendments that we are now debating have already been discussed by the Select Committee but unfortunately were not included in the principal Act. However, Mr Speaker, the spirit of the discussion carried out by the Select Committee were being carried out by officials responsible for implementing the Act. That is why it is possible to make all these refunds at this stage even though the Bill has not been put through the House yet because the matters were discussed and approved by the Select Committee, which I explained were inadvertently omitted from the Act. Therefore, the purpose of the Amendment is to incorporate those omissions in the principal Act. I think the only matter of controversy in this Amendment is removal of the zero-rating in passenger and air freight to the Outer Islands. I believe the Deputy Prime Minister, in his speech yesterday, made the appropriate explanation why that was done. But I would like to go over that explanation again to make it clear to everybody why the zero-rating on passenger and air freight to the Outer Islands has been removed. The idea of zero-rating those items in the first place were so that the shippers and the fliers will be able to reduce fares and freight to the Outer Islands. That was the principle behind zero-rating those items. The idea was that the two concerns were allowed to claim VAT over those commodities, the understanding

being that they will then be able to reduce their charges on freight and passenger fares. Unfortunately, that was not the case even though they could claim a refund on VAT, the fares and the freight did not fall. In fact, Mr Speaker, the shipping freight to the Outer Islands now is at its maximum allowable under the law. By zero-rating it, we expected that cargo freight would drop but unfortunately it remained where it was. At the same time, the firms were claiming a refund on VAT on those air freight and fares.
In other words, Mr Speaker, the benefit of zero-rating those items were being enjoyed by the general public of the Outer Islands. It was felt that the only way control can be made over excessive charges is by price control. Unfortunately, the zero-rating of those items did not provide instruction to the concerned firms to reduce their charges. It was left to the owners of the businesses, left to their discretion to reduce the charges, to take into consideration the reclamation of VAT they were entitled to.
I think I have already mentioned in this House, some time in the past, that there is a move now to bring into the House legislation controlling the unfair practices and overcharging within the business sector. Based on the New Zealand system it is called a Commerce Commission, or something like that. It judges or keeps an eye on unfair practices, something like a Fair Trade Commission. I feel, Mr Speaker, that it is the place to include this zero-rating to control businesses, to bring it in under the umbrella of fair trade legislation. That legislation is now in the Office of the Crown Law and hopefully will be ready quickly to be enacted in this Parliament. Let’s face it, Mr Speaker, there are a lot of businesses that will not survive with competition, although we are for open trade and open competition but there are certain businesses that cannot survive under that scenario. Let us just give an example. One example is international shipping. I know there is a lot of people wanting to compete. Sure, those ones with plenty of money, they can drop their charges down as little as possible in order to get the other out of business. If there are several shipping lines available, the ones with most money will try and get their charges down so that the others will be driven out of business. The danger is once the other line is out of business then the one who has dropped his charges can then raise his charges up. And if his claim for increased charges is not agreed to, he can just say all right we’ll take our ships away. And where does that leave us, Mr Speaker? It leaves us with no shipping line. I use this as an example of disadvantage of certain monopolies. This needs to be looked at urgently because this has actually happened to a shipping line, they couldn’t operate with the reduced charges. They just want to force something else that reduced their charges, they couldn’t survive on that scenario so they asked for an increase and when it wasn’t agreed to they shifted, they moved.
The other scenario, Mr Speaker, is the case of our local airlines, nobody else wishes to compete with it. But we need that local airline over here. That means it’s a Monopoly. But unfortunately nobody else wants to compete with it. At least one company tried to compete, Mr Speaker, but I don’t know what happened to that company. So that is the proposal, Mr Speaker, in order to cover all these possibilities. I am not saying it could happen but they are possibilities. In order to cover those, we need to introduce what I call a Fair Trading Act, or a Commerce Act, or something like that. And that is being drafted right now. And I believe, Mr Speaker, that is the place to incorporate the zero-rating of the outer islands air fares or sea fares
