Oral Question 12

MR SPEAKER: Please be seated. Before we took the adjournment, a question was asked to the Prime Minister and therefore, I give him this opportunity for his answer.
HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker. And I would like to take the opportunity to explain to the Members of this House and to our public the approach that we took in developing this action plan for closer strategic partnership with the people of the Republic of China. And to counter the allegation that this has been done without transparency and even worst by some quarters that this has been done in secret.
Let me refer you, Mr Speaker, to previous agreements that our country signed with New Zealand and with Australia. Those agreements Mr Speaker were signed on the basis of losing our National Sustainable Development Plan as the foundation for areas that we’ve identified as priorities for our country. Consultation, Mr Speaker was very heavily evident in the development of our NSDP. This is not the Government plan Mr Speaker but rather this is the Cook Islands’ people plan.
The lengthy consultation that took place to develop our NSDP involved the NGO groups, the private sector, government agencies, traditional leaders, the RAC and numerous other Cook Islands community groups. In this development plan, Mr Speaker, there’s “kaveinga nui” is the

voice and the choice of our people. When we come to the development of our agreements with other countries. The first one with New Zealand, this was done in 2022. The two governments had a meeting, and it was determined the areas the two can work in partnership. And it was written in a contract agreement, it is called the ‘Vaka Haurua’ or the ‘Vaka Purua’ – double hull canoe.
The two governments signed the contract agreement and then given to the public, the people. There was no measure of consultation in the development and the negotiation of that agreement because the voice of the people was captured in the foundation document that we used to negotiate with New Zealand and that was our NSDP.
The same with Australia, later that same year 2022, the two-government met responsible for the foreign affairs division in their different countries. They discussed and they identified and determined the areas where both governments can work together. It was returned to our Cabinet and received and accepted by our Cabinet. The two governments signed the contract and it was released to the public. Both of these documents are right before me now, Mr Speaker, easily available online.
And if anybody is interested, it sets out the areas of cooperation between our countries with New Zealand and with Australia. It is from this documents, Mr Speaker that will emanate the key specific projects that our two countries will enter into. This provides the framework, Mr Speaker, for areas of cooperation and collaboration with these two countries. This is exactly the same approach we have taken with the People’s Republic of China. We have made no secret of the fact that we want to enter into a strategic partnership agreement. And as I have mentioned in previous communications in the media, this is something that we have been in discussion with our key partners since last year.
Our officials have been in formal and informal discussions with their counterparts over the last year. In our previous agreement we signed, Mr Speaker, we did not share the details of our Vaka Haurua, Vaka Purua with the government of Australia. And we did not share the agreements we were in discussion with Australia or Tumanava agreement with New Zealand. But until the two governments were happy with the final document and they were signed off, then we were able to share these documents.
I can confirm with our team that the discussions and negotiations on our part on the final language and the final wordings in parts of our agreement were reached only on the day of the actual signing with our comprehensive strategic partnership with China. To correct the misinformation that is going around on social media by commentators here, that this agreement was already signed before we even left to go to China was totally wrong. And to answer the question of the Leader of the Opposition, as a lawyer herself, she should be aware that how can you be transparent about an agreement that it still under negotiation and even yet to be signed.
But I can say, Mr Speaker, that within this agreement, there is one area that we should be proud of with our officials in their negotiations on the final wording of this document. And this is a concession that the People’s Republic of China agreed to and that is at any project that does take place under this agreement within the Cook Islands shall fall under Cook Islands law. Furthermore, it shall follow the procurement policies of the Cook Islands. This Mr Speaker is a
