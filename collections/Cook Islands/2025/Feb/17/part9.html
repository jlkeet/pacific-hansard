We have two Ministerial Statements today. I call the Honourable Prime Minister, Mark Brown; you have the Floor.
HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker. Kia Orana to everyone today here in this Honourable House, including our people listening in online.
As we gather today for the first Sitting of the Parliament year, we do so at the historic moment. This year, we commemorate 60 years of self-governance, a milestone that reflects the resilience, the vision and the determination of our people.
We have experienced many milestones, and achievements in our journey as a nation, from the visionary determination of our ai tupuna to enter into an arrangement of self-governance in free association with New Zealand in 1965. The building of the Rarotonga airport, to the economic hardships of 1997 financial crisis, and the subsequent rebuilding of our nation in the decades that followed. We endured the devastating impact of COVID-19 where we closed our borders for two years. Yet, we emerged with an economy that is recovered stronger than ever, reaffirming our place as the first Pacific Island nation to achieve high income status.
These defining moments remind us of an undeniable truth. We are a nation that navigate our own future with vision, with grace, and with determination. We have never been afraid to made the bold decisions. To forge our own path and to take control of our destiny. That is why today, as we mark the beginning of this Parliamentary year, I stand before you with the same commitment, to lead with confidence, clarity and conviction in the best interest of our Cook Islands people.

Mr Speaker, our economy continues to evolve. Tourism remains the backbone of our prosperity, bringing in over 170 thousand visitors in 2024, a near record number. But we must not rely on a single sector to drive our development agenda. The lesson we learnt through the experience of COVID-19, is that we must diversify our economic landscape. Diversification of our economy is critical to ensure our economic resilience and sustainable development.
We are actively developing our seabed mineral sector under a world leading regulatory framework, ensuring that we strike the necessary balance between responsible resource management and environment protection. Our financial services sector continues to provide a strong foundation for growth, bringing in new opportunities for finance and investment.
Alongside these efforts and through our international partnerships, we are expanding access to global market positioning the Cook Islands as a leader in sustainable development and economic innovation. Our recent engagement including with China, New Zealand, Australia, and the United States, reflect our strategic approach; one of openness, collaboration and sovereign decision making. We can choose to be a country that relies on handouts and is dependent on our big brother, or we can choose to be a country that can stand on its own two feet and can engage in constructive partnerships of mutual benefit.
We have been through a lot together Mr Speaker, over the past 60 years, we have fought for our place in this world, and we have built a nation that stands tall, proud and sober. And we have done that as Cook Islanders. Our country stands as a beacon to many others in the Pacific region. Let us be proud of who we are and what we have achieved as a nation, and as a people.
Mr Speaker, our journey as a self-governing nation is not one of isolation but of partnership and engagement. As we look outward, we re-affirm our commitment to multilateral agreements. A recent example is the Paris Agreement and our Regional climate leadership.
Climate change remains one of our greatest extra special challenges and we will not stand idle while larger nationals debate their responsibilities. The Cook Islands will continue to lead climate action, advocating for stronger commitments from the global partners while securing vital resources to support our resilience. In this time of change with the USA again pulling out of the Paris Agreement and its commitments, the Pacific countries now look to other countries to fill the gap and do more. Therefore, Mr Speaker it was reassuring in my discussions with Premier Liu that China will step up in terms of meeting their carbon emissions targets and also in supporting small islands states to help build resilience against the impacts of climate change.
At the same time, Mr Speaker we are strengthening economic diplomacy through initiatives like our comprehensive strategic partnership with China, which provides structured opportunities in trade, infrastructure and the blue economy. This does not replace our long-standing relationships with New Zealand, Australia and others but rather it compliments them ensuring that we have a diversified portfolio of partnerships.

Mr Speaker, in 1965, we, the people of the Cook Islands exercised our right for self-determination and entered into a relationship of free association with New Zealand. At that time our people had four governance choices to choose from:
1.To go independent; 2.To remain a colony of New Zealand; 3.To explore Polynesian Federation with our near neighbours Tahiti or; 4.Self-governance in free association.
Mr Speaker with great foresight and historical and ancestry ties that bound us to Aotearoa, our people chose self-governing with free-association with New Zealand and so was born 60 years ago the state of the Cook Islands as we know ourselves to be today. That relationship of free- association with New Zealand has remained an evolving one of partnership, freely entered into and freely maintained with both countries respecting the right and freedom of the other to pursue their own national policies and interest.
Mr Speaker, since 1965 under successive governments and in cooperation with many development partners, the Cook Islands has developed in many ways. Consonant with its growing experience and expertise in foreign affairs matters the Cook Islands have increasingly assumed direct responsibility for our own international relations, bilateral and multilateral, regional and global. Those three-association arrangement that had been codified by the Cook Islands and New Zealand in successive understanding over the decades including the 2001 Joint-Centenary Declaration.
Mr Speaker, to date the Cook Islands has established diplomatic relations and formal partnership with 66 countries and European Union, we are members of over 40 organisations, including most UN specialized agencies and are a party to over 150 multilateral treaties, conventions and protocols in our own right.
