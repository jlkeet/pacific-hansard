HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker, probably more appropriate that I answer the Honourable Members question because it is really related to finance. Not to disrespect the Deputy Prime Minister to suggest that he has no idea of finance. Our initial estimates for the cost of Manihiki airport as provided to us by our consultant firm ACOM and others in New Zealand indicated that if private company was to conduct this work, Mr Speaker, the cost will be prohibited.
In fact, the cost will be two to three times more than if the government took on this job itself because there is cost associated with private contractors, Mr Speaker, that are not associated with government contract. What we have done, Mr Speaker, is turn back the clock to a model that suits small island economies like ours to be able to carry out large infrastructure cost projects. As I have said before, Mr Speaker, it doesn’t matter if you are sealing an airport for a hundred people or a hundred thousand people, the cost is still the same.
But that cost when you divide it over a smaller population means that we pay disproportionally high cost for our infrastructure projects as small island states. And we found that if the government were to take on this project using what we call the public works model.
Our airport built back in the day use this public works model where we had engineers like George Cowan and others and the government public works carrying out the work. Our Ministry of Finance and the procurement team has found that utilising this model again, would give significant savings to the project.
The first point to consider is that a private company not only needs to cover the project's costs but also must include a margin for profit. For a large, one-time project that involves significant capital investment in machinery and equipment, this profit margin can be quite substantial. Therefore, we have determined that by adopting a public works model, we can execute the necessary work to the required standards while staying within the budget allocations we have available to us, Mr. Speaker.
