Oral Question 22

HON. V. MOKOROA: Thank you, Mr Speaker, and Kia Orana to you and all Members. The question raised concerns whether the Ministry of Education has a plan to preserve our language. However, I believe this question would be better directed to the Ministry of Cultural Development, as they oversee a division focused on the Maori language under the leadership of Raemaki Karati.
Moreover, the Ministry of Education is actively encouraging students from Grade 1 to Grade 6 to continue speaking the Maori language. I want to emphasise that we have various dialects and languages, and when children from Nga-Pu-Toru come to Rarotonga, their dialect may be overshadowed, as teachers in Rarotonga will primarily teach the Rarotonga dialect in the classroom. I would like us to consider the example set by the island of Pukapuka, where they successfully translated the Maori Bible into their own language. This serves as encouragement for us not to

rely solely on the Ministry of Education, but rather to challenge each individual island to undertake similar initiatives, such as translating books like the Bible into their own dialects and languages.
Furthermore, each household should be motivated to teach and speak the Cook Islands Maori language at home. We must acknowledge that in the 1970s and 80s, children faced punishment for speaking our Cook Islands Maori language in schools, often subjected to physical discipline for not adhering to language rules. So, who can we truly blame today for our children not speaking our mother tongue? Let us not allow this to become a significant issue for us. As our Chaplain reminded us this afternoon, especially to us Honourable Members, we are the light of the nation.
It is our responsibility to develop policies and initiatives that encourage the use of our Cook Islands language, not just in classrooms but in all aspects of our daily lives so that you know who you are. Thank you, Mr Speaker.
