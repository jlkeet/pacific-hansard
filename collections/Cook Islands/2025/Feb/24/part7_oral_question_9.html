HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker. I would like to thank the Members and mostly to the Members from the Opposition for giving their support to the Supplementary Budget. Mr Speaker, I am amazed to hear the Member from Akaoa saying that we have a lot of money appropriated to the Pa Enua and the Member from Tamarua is saying that a lot of money is being appropriated on Rarotonga. So, I leave it to the Members to come to an agreement so I can approve their requests.
I would like to revisit the topic of our vessel, Mr Speaker. We acknowledge that $3 million has been allocated for this purpose. It is important to note that we have been working on securing this vessel for over four years, ensuring that it is appropriate and suitable for transporting cargo and a limited number of passengers to the Pa Enua.
We were very firm, Mr Speaker, with both the Government of Japan and the Government of China regarding the specifications we required for our interisland vessel. I am pleased that our team took the necessary time to ensure we did not simply accept any vessel that was offered to us. Instead, we insisted on a vessel that would fulfill our requirements for long-term operation and low maintenance. We prioritised a design that would keep operating costs manageable and, most

The challenge, Mr Speaker, is that there are not many ocean-going vessels of this size available. When we look at our neighboring countries like Tonga and Samoa, their populations exceed 150,000 to 250,000 people, 10 times our own population. Consequently, their shipping needs are tenfold, requiring significantly more freight and cargo capacity, as well as the ability to transport many more passengers to their Pa Enua. This explains why we see larger ships, such as the Lady Naomi and others, navigating our waters.
However, we simply cannot sustain vessels of that size, which is why we must explore smaller vessels that can effectively accommodate our cargo volumes and the number of passengers we need to transport.
I know, Mr. Speaker, that the people in the Pa Enua, along with the Members of Parliament representing them, are eagerly looking forward to a high-quality shipping service that adequately meets their needs.
Mr Speaker, this is one of the key requirements for us to try and grow our economy by ensuring that each of our islands can contribute to the Cook Islands economy. It is not just about shipping, Mr Speaker; we also need to focus on our airports, specifically the necessity of sealing our runways.
The Member from Akaoa raised a crucial point regarding Aitutaki, which now handles up to 30 flights a week. This success is a result of the infrastructure we have put in place to facilitate such growth. Without the runway sealing we completed 20 years ago, we would still be relying on Bandit aircraft to reach Aitutaki. Mr Speaker, the vision of this government is clear, by investing in infrastructure, when we do this infrastructure work, because we have seen it happen – the growth that happened in Aitutaki we will give the opportunity now for other islands to be able to seize on that opportunity and grow their abilities and their opportunities.
What are we doing to encourage the outer islands to participate in our economy, Mr Speaker? We are implementing the very same initiatives that the Opposition Members are critical of, such as the $60,000 tax-free threshold. These incentives, Mr Speaker, are designed to attract motivated individuals to work and produce in the outer islands, as they can retain all their income until they exceed $60,000. For hardworking couples, this means a combined income of up to $120,000 before any taxes become applicable. Those who are driven and see the opportunity, Mr Speaker, will certainly take advantage of this.
Members have highlighted the potential for planting and exporting crops here in Rarotonga, noting that individuals can do so up to a value of $160,000 without incurring income tax, Mr Speaker. This government is actively seeking ways to empower people to improve their own circumstances. However, we must also consider the situation in Rarotonga. A visit to any of our Pa Enua makes it clear just how challenging life can be there. The further you go, the tougher it gets. In the Northern Group, you do not know if you are going to get a boat in the next three, four, five months.

Looking back 60 years, Mr Speaker, attending functions such as bereavements or weddings meant that if you wanted to bring food, you had to catch a homegrown chicken. Nowadays, the price of chicken in Rarotonga is quite affordable, about $30 a carton. People can easily buy one, two, or even 10 cartons to take along. However, in the Outer Islands, the situation is very different. A carton of chicken can cost $80, and at times, there may not even be any available because the boat did not deliver the goods.
So, when we complain about our life here in Rarotonga, we must remember the life of our people in the Pa Enua.
We have a good water system over here but maybe not so good in Ruaau but in the Pa Enua, especially in the north again, one of our islands in the south, water is a struggle. I know that our Pa Enua people wish that they had a water system like our one here in Rarotonga. So, let’s not forget that the policy of this government is that no one in the tribe will be left behind – no one left out.
The Appropriation, Mr Speaker, dedicated to road improvements, is a long-awaited initiative. I understand that these funds will be utilised for the back road in Nikao. However, we also have outstanding projects in Titikaveka and Puaikura that need to be addressed. This Supplementary Budget aims to expedite the construction of key roads, allowing us to seal all our roads as quickly as possible. This will help ensure that the roads meet the high standards that our people have come to expect here in Rarotonga.
Regarding the comments that our budget is unusual, Mr Speaker, as I mentioned in my Budget Speech, the additional $68 million may initially appear to be a significant and excessive amount. Let me again put that into perspective, Mr Speaker. Of the $68 million, $19 million is being carried forward from the previous year’s appropriation. A substantial portion of this amount is due to timing of infrastructure projects. This is not new money, as you would say; rather, it is unspent money that needs to be carried forward to this year and utilised this year.
Another significant amount, Mr Speaker, is the $40 million allocated for debt servicing. During the COVID years, particularly in 2020, the government tapped into its entire economic stabilisation fund of $78 million and also accessed over $150 million in New Zealand grant funding. However, we still needed to incur debt, Mr Speaker, through the Asian Development Bank, which was essential for keeping our businesses afloat. Furthermore, since we graduated to high-income status in 2020, the debt we acquired from the ADB was at commercial rates, which means we are no longer eligible for concessional financing typically available to a country like us.
What we have done, Mr Speaker, is that as these debts have now become due, four years after their inception, we have successfully repaid a considerable amount, totaling approximately $40 million. This achievement has allowed us to reduce the anticipated debt servicing costs for the next four to five years to a more manageable level. Consequently, we can now allocate funds to critical areas such as welfare and addressing cost of living increases. This exemplifies good, sound financial management. By adhering to the principles of effective financial management and the medium- term budgeting outlined in our Supplementary Budget, we anticipate that our country will return
