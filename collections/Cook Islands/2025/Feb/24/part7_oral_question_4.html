Mr Speaker, I would like to speak as a former Secretary for the Ministry of Cultural Development, particularly regarding the necessity of hiring and chartering ships to transport our people to Rarotonga for Te Maeva Nui and back. In the early 2000s to 2010, our only option was to charter local shipping. It is fair to say that waiting on the wharf for those ships to arrive was often a difficult experience. As the individual responsible for chartering these vessels, I felt a significant responsibility toward the well-being, safety, and health of the passengers.
Many times, ships coming from the Northern Group Islands would arrive with a quarter of the passengers needing urgent medical attention for pneumonia. We all know how passengers often ended up staying in makeshift tents on the deck for three or four days, enduring harsh weather, high seas, and relentless sea spray. As a result, many would fall ill, necessitating ambulances to take them straight to the hospital upon arrival. Often, they had been wet at sea for two or three days, leading to their vulnerable state.
Having witnessed these conditions firsthand, it became apparent that we needed to seek better, safer shipping options for our people. This need led us to explore alternatives like the Lady Naomi from Samoa and Tahiti Nui from French Polynesia. Continuing to rely on local shipping under those harsh conditions was simply unreasonable. It was unfair to expect our people to endure such hardships, struggle to recover, and then perform at Te Maeva Nui.
I understand that there are many comments regarding the shipping situation, particularly concerning the Chinese vessel. While there are also Japanese ships in the mix, the current focus seems to be on the Chinese ship. It is important to note that those making these complaints have likely never experienced travel on the interisland vessels. They have not faced the difficult conditions that passengers endure, nor have they suffered illness while aboard these ships.
Mr Speaker, I completely and wholeheartedly support this Vote Item that our government has undertaken to enhance the safety of shipping for our Pa Enua.
I recall the tragic incident when a ferry sank in Tonga, resulting in the loss of nearly 100 lives, as well as the Rabaul Queen in Papua New Guinea, which caught fire while docked, leading to further fatalities. In the aftermath of these disasters, regulations and shipping laws within the Pacific were strengthened to prevent similar tragedies across the region and within our own country. Therefore, we have no option but to enhance and progress the conditions of our Pa Enua shipping. For this reason, I support this Item in the amendment Budget.
Mr Speaker, I would also like to address public safety governance, specifically the police initiative related to the $145,000 allocated for Community Policing. We gathered volunteers from our villages to launch the Community Policing initiative, with strong support and coordination from our Police Department. While I support this Item, I would like to take a moment, on behalf of my fellow Members of Parliament, Rob, Kaka, and myself, to thank the volunteers who responded to our call to help kickstart our community policing efforts.

HON. T. PUPUKE BROWNE: Kia Orana to all you this afternoon. I just wanted to say a few words on the merits of this Bill because later we will have the opportunity to go through each Vote Item.
The purpose of a Supplementary Appropriation should be to cater for unforeseen revenue receipts or expenditure not identified from the original appropriation. You would not expect there to be any significant changes unless there has been a disaster or unforeseen circumstances. This Appropriation Amendment is not that. It is not merely a routine financial adjustment; it is much more than that.
With the 2025/26 Budget set to be presented in a few months’ time, the scale of this Supplementary Budget is therefore quite alarming. The Bill increases total government expenditure from $328.3 million to $396.3 million, an extraordinary $68 million increase.
Now, government claims that this is due to increased revenue. Its spending decisions however, indicate a prioritisation of political self-interest over public interest.
In the presentation that the Opposition received from MFEM, and we are grateful, I have to acknowledge we are grateful for the presentation because we are a little bit more educated than we were yesterday.
One of the key things that they were pushing is that the increased budget was to address critical priorities and it is this very definition of “critical priorities” that I want to comment on. Of course, previous speakers have spoken about the increased amount for Members of Parliament travel and the amount of the increase is about $450,000, and when you look at that amount and then you look at the amount that is allocated for the Police, it makes absolutely no sense.
