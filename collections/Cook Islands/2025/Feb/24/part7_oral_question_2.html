Oral Question 2

MR R. HEATHER: Kia Orana to all Honourable Members this afternoon. I have a Supplementary Question related to the issue raised by the Member for Rangiatea regarding the allocation for the Police. I would like to commend the government for increasing the appropriation to the Police, enabling them to carry out their responsibilities effectively until the end of the financial year. However, as noted by the Member, this amount may still not be sufficient.
Thank you to the staff of the Ministry of Finance and Economic Management for their guidance to the PAC committee on how we can enhance this aspect of our budget. They have assured us that there will be improvements to the Police budget in the upcoming main budget. As a member of the PAC committee, I support this appropriation and would like to share some of my thoughts regarding this supplementary matter.
Regarding government spending, I would like to comment on the appropriation allocated to Members of Parliament for overseas travel allowances. It is worth noting that there has been a significant increase in the budget for these travel allowances over the years. In our meeting with MFEM, they described this budget category as a "critical priority area."

Regarding the airport mentioned by the Honourable Member for Mangaia, we heard about the government's plans for the airport in Manihiki, including contributions from our development partners and support from our government. A year has already passed, and we are still waiting for progress on the Manihiki airport project. I do not wish to undermine the people of Manihiki, as they are also my family. I simply want to share my thoughts with the House.
In 1971, the population of Manihiki was around 200 people. Considering that the government plans to spend over $23 million on the airport, this amounts to approximately $102,000 per person when distributed among the population. This raises the question on what will the return on investment be for the entire nation? We are all aware that only one plane flies to Manihiki every fortnight. In contrast, for the island of Aitutaki, we have invested about $3.4 million to improve their airstrip, which sees more than 30 flights a week from Rarotonga.
Through this, we can clearly see the benefits we are reaping from the tourists visiting Aitutaki, as well as the foods that are transported there. The question I want to raise today draws from what the Member from Mangaia mentioned, highlighting a significant problem and concern on the island of Mangaia.
I believe this is also a significant issue on the island of Atiu, underscoring the urgent need for improvements to their airports. Those are the thoughts I wish to present before this House. Thank you.

My question is this, given that there are insufficient funds to meet all our needs, particularly in the crucial area of agriculture, how do we plan to address this significant requirement for our country?
Over 170,000 tourists have visited our country, highlighting the urgent need for support in agriculture. I speak from a place of understanding that our planters are unable to supply enough produce to meet the demands of our hotels and accommodations for tourists. I believe this is a crucial area where the economic benefits will not merely go to business owners but will instead directly support our own people.
Regarding the island of Mangaia, we recognise the efforts of one of our young men, Arama Wigmore, who has been cultivating produce on Mangaia and bringing it to Rarotonga. My question is, what is the government doing to support the transportation of agricultural produce from the Outer Islands to facilitate sales in Rarotonga?
There are other regions in our country that are eager to support Rarotonga, but I have observed that the government is not providing the necessary assistance for them to offer services in this area. We recognise that Air Rarotonga needs to generate profit to sustain its operations, and similarly, our two local shipping companies also require support. It is essential that the government actively supports these services.
There is another important area I would like us to consider. Many of our people are constructing small huts to provide accommodations for visiting tourists, both in Rarotonga and the Outer Islands. I empathise with those who have invested significant amounts of their money in this endeavour, yet there is a noticeable lack of support from our government to assist them. I believe that government employees who travel to the Outer Islands should consider staying in these small business accommodations, as it would benefit both our local economy and the community.
Each year, we allocate a significant portion of our national budget to tourism, seemingly prioritising investments to attract tourists to our country. However, we must consider our own people who have invested in their holiday homes and other local developments. I strongly support the Member for Akaoa in highlighting the amount of money being directed towards the airport development in Manihiki. While that is important, we must also acknowledge that there are other areas and islands in our country that similarly require government support.
When examining this situation, it seems that the government is not prioritising financial support for our own people, who could benefit from it, but rather focusing on external interests. I also want to point out, Mr Speaker, that our people in Rarotonga appear to be penalised by the current tax structure. For those in the Outer Islands, once they reach an income threshold of $60,000, they begin to pay taxes, while Rarotonga contributes significantly more yet faces disadvantages. Although the government offers incentives to the people of the Pa Enua, many continue to leave their islands.
