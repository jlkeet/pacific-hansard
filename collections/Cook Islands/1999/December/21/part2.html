MR SPEAKER took the Chair at 1.00 p.m.
MR SPEAKER: Please be seated Honourable Members, Parliament is now resumed.
First, I would like to thank the Honourable Member for Titikaveka Hon. Robert Wigmore for leading our devotions this afternoon.
Secondly, I would like to take this opportunity to welcome all to the sitting this afternoon in the belief it will be the last sitting for this year.
Thirdly, I have a letter from the Honourable Member for the Overseas Constituency Dr Joseph Williams. Yesterday, we heard verbally about his condition but now we have a letter to confirm his situation.
“Dear Sir, As a result of a surgery to my eye, I have been rendered unfit to travel. I am therefore unable to attend Parliament at this last meeting for 1999. I seek your indulgence and that of all Members of Parliament for leave of absence.
May I take this opportunity to wish you and your staff and all Honourable Members of Parliament a very Merry Christmas and a Happy New Year. As we enter the New Year, I extend very best wishes to all Cook Islanders for a happy, prosperous millennium. I do so on behalf of my wife and all Cook Islanders in the Overseas Constituency. We pray for unity, peace and prosperity of our small nation. God Bless. Dr Joseph Williams."
Thank you for your indulgence Honourable Members.

SIR G.A. HENRY, K.B.E. Kia Orana to you Mr Speaker. Greetings to you and every one of us at Parliament. SIR G.A. HENRY, K.B.E. Thank you Mr Speaker. Why is there a need to move this Motion and overlook the need in this instance for this Bill to be translated into Maori so that we can all read it in English and Maori? People might want to buy a copy and read it in both Maori and English? You see this Bill is going to affect the lives of our people in Rarotonga and elsewhere in the Cook Islands because Mr Speaker we buy a lot of foodstuffs from overseas. Every shop, every person who orders goods from overseas is going to be affected by this Bill. Every person who eats tinned fish, tinned corn beef, tinned peaches, spaghetti, a whole series of stuff you find on the shelves of every shop, every person who takes things off the shelf of any shop is going to be affected by this Bill. Why can’t give this Bill be given a bit of time in order for us all to understand exactly what it is about? Why is it not being translated into Maori, what is the rush?

I have a very good idea, Mr Speaker, that the Ministers did not get to read this Bill until this morning. I doubt that they have read it. I doubt Mr Speaker, whether Members of the Caucus have even read the first page.
HON. N. GEORGE: Yes, we spent a whole morning on it.

It is important for the country therefore this is going to take time out to think over this thing through carefully.
I have no doubt, Mr Speaker that later on the Honourable Dr Pupuke Robati is going to get up and say “in your time you used to do this too”. If it’s not him it’s likely to be the Honourable Member, Norman George, maybe the Honourable Dr Maoate, the Prime Minister and maybe the Honourable Member, Dr Woonton. They are going to get up and say, “you used to do this so why can’t we do it?”.
Over a period of ten years when we were in Mr Speaker, slightly more than ten, there may have been one or two Bills that we rushed through this House urgently. But this crowd hasn’t been in for two minutes. They haven’t been in for two minutes and they are already rushing the Bill through. Mr Speaker, I don’t know that in terms of accountability to our people, in terms of accountability to those in the private sector, to all who are buying things from the shops, and to all who import things from New Zealand and are reliant upon international shipping, are we doing the right thing for them.
I believe, Mr Speaker, that it is appropriate that this Bill be translated into Maori. I have heard no explanation from the Minister in Charge that gives me some sense of comfort to agree with having this Bill dealt with solely in the English language. I am sure that we altogether sense our responsibility to those that voted us into this House, enough for us to want to do the right thing for them. Is it right therefore that a Bill as important as this be proceeded upon not in the Maori language? Is it right that we should rush it through? My guess is that maybe the Ministers have had an opportunity to look through it just this morning. I have no doubt that the Bill has been in action in terms of it’s been constructed for quite some time. But I am absolutely certain that it was only this morning that the Ministers, not to mention the Members of the Caucus, had their first opportunity, Mr Speaker, to read this document. Because I got confirmation about it from the Prime Minister yesterday and from the Honourable Dr Pupuke Robati. As of yesterday at the time when we adjourned Parliament none of them had seen this document. So maybe they got it last night. I doubt it. I suspect they got it for the first time this morning.
