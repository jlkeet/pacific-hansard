Constitution Amendment (No.24) Bill, Second Reading. The Honourable Prime Minister.
HON. SIR G.A. HENRY: Greetings, Mr Speaker, and Greetings to all Honourable Members in the House whom the people of this country have elected to be their representatives in this House. Greetings also to the people listening in to our debate this evening. Greetings to you all in the name of Our Lord.
This Amendment Bill in the House, Mr Speaker, has come about through a recommendation of the Political Review Commission. The Commission sought the views of the community through the meetings they held and the questionnaires they gave them. It became apparent, Mr Speaker, that only a few people answered the question with regards to shortening the term of Parliament. It was discovered that some people thought a five year term was fine while some wanted four, and there were also those who wanted to drop it to three. The purpose of this Amendment Bill in front of us, Mr Speaker, is to present our people, before the general election, the choice of a term of Parliament of four years. If you agree, you will find a box on the ballot paper, which says ‘yes’. If you do not accept that it be shortened, you can tick the ‘no’ box. If you wish the Parliamentary term to remain at a five year term, you can mark in the box that you do not agree.
There is a rather strange requirement in the Constitution, which states that if there is to be any change in the term of Parliament it must first be presented to the people. This is an amendment made by the Democratic Party Government at the time when they decided Parliament should be extended to five years from the original four

What this means is, the current Government cannot change the term of any Parliament without first going to the people in a referendum. The people will decide whether they want to support a change to the term of Parliament or not. Therefore, Mr Speaker, I am happy to move:
That this Constitution Amendment (No. 24) Bill be now Read a Second time
We do not have to decide in this House whether we stand for four or five years, you don’t have to do that. We do not wish as Members of Parliament to influence the decision of our people on the term of Parliament, whether it be reduced to four years or keep it at five. The requirement of this Amendment is to give our people the right to make a decision either to reduce the term of Parliament to four years or keep it at five years. As I see it, Mr Speaker, our job in Parliament right now is to accept the Bill before us, without going into Committee, to give the people the opportunity to choose yes or no.
This is the idea, Mr Speaker. If a Member wishes to talk on the principle and merits, that is fine, no one will stop you, but we won’t go into the Committee of the Whole to scrutinise each Clause of the Bill. I think, after the debate on the principles and merits, we will stop there. We will let the people make the decision. Thank you.

I showed them and read to them the recommendations made by the Political Review Commission and the Constitution Amendment No.23 Bill, which we debated on. This is what my people have decided, three years - not four years. I asked them the reason why they decided on three years, they replied ‘so we don’t have to suffer like we are doing today - it will be shorter - it will be three years of suffering not five years!’. If the going is good then it will be easy to bring that Government back after three years. I thought about this and I realised they were wise.
