HON. P. POKINO: Mr Speaker, Greetings to you, to all your staff Members, Honourable Members in the House and to our people who are listening in to the debate this afternoon, Greetings to you all.
Mr Speaker, I move:
That the 1998/99 Appropriation Amendment Bill be Read a Second time
Mr Speaker we must realise the Supplementary Appropriation is always brought on the first few week of the month of June. The idea being, at that stage the Supplementary Estimates reflect the actual expenditure of Government for the year because the main budget which is always introduced in July reflects the projections made by Ministries as to revenue Government intends to collect together with proposed expenditure. But this year as we all know, it is impossible for us to bring in the Supplementary Estimates at the end of June because under the Constitution, Parliament is due to be prorogued by the 24th of March this year. Yes, because that is the day five years ago that the last general election was held. So another general election is due this year. Thus, Parliament must rise by the 24th of March. Therefore, it is necessary to bring this amendment in today because as we all know the procedures of the estimates require ten days in the Committee stage before it can be passed. So, although the Supplementary would have reflected the actual figures up to the end of February it will have to rely on projections for the months of March, April, May and June.
Mr Speaker it is my honour and pleasure to present the 1998-1999 Appropriation Amendment Bill. This Appropriation Amendment Bill was foreshadowed in my 1998-1999 Appropriation Bill speech and incorporates the year end tidying up the government’s financial books.
In view of the coming Election, it is unlikely that Parliament will be able to consider a further Appropriation Bill before the end of the financial year.
An essential strategy announced in the 1998-99 Appropriation Bill was the planned debt restructuring programme. I am pleased to advise, Mr Speaker, that the Government has been successfully renegotiating the major debt with Italy, Nauru and New Zealand.
The agreements marked the end of negotiations which have been both lengthy and complex and which culminated in a meeting with the major creditors of the Cook Islands Government in Manila in September last year.

Prior to the debt restructuring, the total face value of the Cook Islands interest bearing debt including the claims by Italy and all other creditors was nearly $200 million. Because of write-offs in the agreements, the face value of the debt reduced to $127 million. If the value of the additional concessions granted such as extended repayment terms and low interest rates are taken into account the debt reduces to $77 million.
Unfortunately, it has not been possible to reach agreement regarding restructuring of the debt owed to France but negotiations are continuing and the Government remains hopeful of a satisfactory resolution.
A significant change in the presentation of the Estimates is the replacement of the Schedule “budget at a glance” with “statement of fiscal responsibility”. This Statement has been included to avoid any misunderstanding as to whether the Budget meets the fiscally responsible guidelines in accordance with Section 23 of the MFEM Act 1995-96.
I am pleased to report that the key indicator for fiscal responsibility of ensuring that total operating expenses of the Crown in each financial year are not more than its total operating revenues - has been achieved. The original budget shortfall of $1.27 million has been reduced to a break-even level.
This, Mr Speaker, shows the wisdom of this Government by not acting hastily and making drastic cuts in the public sector staffing and services provided as some would have wished us to have done when the original Appropriation was tabled.
I am also delighted to report Mr Speaker, that the Government has made significant progress towards the second key indicator of achieving and maintaining levels of Crown net worth that provide a buffer against factors that may impact adversely on the Crown’s net worth in the future.
The debt restructuring referred to earlier have substantially reduced major debt and ensured that repayments of this debt during the early years will be at a level that can be sustained and will assist in the recovery of the economy. Current indications are that our growth will be higher than anticipated when negotiating the debt repayment agreement and any increase in the growth of the economy will be for the advantage of all Cook Islanders and not just our creditors as has been the case in the past.
Total taxation revenue remains unchanged from the original budget of $34.4 million. There is, however, a reduction of $1.3 million for import levies matched by corresponding increase in company tax. The original budget for total taxation revenue was at the time the Budget was prepared indicated an optimistic forecast. I am pleased to report that this optimistic target is becoming more achievable as the year progresses.
I would like to add Mr Speaker, that the new tax package was introduced in 1997 and as forecasted at the time it will take the country time to realise the benefits of that tax package. It appears now that the expected benefit is now beginning to be realised. I would like to add that the new tax package was introduced in 1997 and as forecasted

The Government’s contribution to the New Zealand Superannuation Scheme for those public servants who still belong to the scheme is reduced by the trustees of the scheme from 11.2 per cent to 7.2 per cent of salary. This has meant a reduction in the Ministry’s budget of some $370,000. In the original budget dividends of $3 million were anticipated from contributions from the CIIC. Sadly this will not be achieved. The major contributor to this dividend was the repayment of the loan for the restructuring of the Rarotongan Hotel. While this will still be received there is doubt that it will be received by 30th June. Included in the Appropriation Amendment Bill is an operational dividend of $400,000 from Telecom Cook Islands. This has been confirmed by Telecom and agreed by CIIC that when it is received a dividend to the Crown of the same amount will be declared.
A new item in the Appropriation Amendment is the sum of $158,000 for interests received on Crown funds. There has always been interest received but generally of a significant nature. This has been achieved through changes in banking arrangements and in the way the investment of surplus funds is managed.
Finally, Mr Speaker, I will repeat my closing words when I introduced the original Budget Bill. The Government is committed to continuing the tight financial management that has been in progress over the past two years.
Government is also conscious of the urgent need to grow the economy, as we are however, restricted by limited resources and it is Government’s duty to allocate those limited resources equitably, and to take into account the cultural, social and economic impacts while doing so. To concentrate on one at the expense of the others will make our Nation a poorer one.
Mr Speaker, I have much pleasure in commending this Appropriation Bill to you.
Thank you.
