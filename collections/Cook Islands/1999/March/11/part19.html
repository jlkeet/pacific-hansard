Constitution Amendment (No. 25) Bill Second Reading. The Honourable Prime Minister.
HON. SIR G.A. HENRY: Thank you. Mr Speaker I move:
That Constitution Amendment (No. 25) be now Read a Second time
MR SPEAKER: Before I call upon the Honourable Minister let us complete the Third Reading of the Constitution Amendment (No.23) Bill.
CLERK OF PARLIAMENT: A Bill intituled: An Act to amend the Constitution.
MR SPEAKER: This concludes the Third reading of the Constitution Amendment (No.23) Bill.
HON. SIR G.A. HENRY: Thank you, Mr Speaker. Constitution Amendment (No.25) Mr Speaker contains only three clauses. That being the case, I am expecting that it will not be necessary for you to tell the Leader of the Opposition to sit down.
This Bill is seeking to give effect to two of the recommendations made by the Political Review Commission, which in my view, are worthy of the support of this Parliament. Before I speak of them, I ought to confirm that prior to his leaving for Aitutaki, the Leader of the New Alliance Party did tell me that I can be rest assured for his support for Constitution Amendment (No. 24) and this Amendment.
And I should also confirm Mr Speaker, that the Leader of the Democratic Alliance Party also gave me his assurance that he would support Amendments No.24 and No.25.
HON. DR T. MAOATE: I have changed my mind since I have been told to shut up.
HON. SIR G.A. HENRY: I have always known him to be a free agent anyway, Mr Speaker.
One of the suggestions that came through and quite strongly in the Political Reform Report was the establishment of a National Appointments Council to vet the appointment of people who hold judicial and semi-judicial positions. The intention is to lend as much impartiality as possible to the appointment of these high positions such as the Chief Justice, the Ombudsman, the Commissioner of Police, the Solicitor-General, etc., to lend their appointment as much impartiality as is possible.

The clear view, which is what I support, is that positions such as these should be free of any politics to whatever extent is possible under human control. People appointed to these positions will then achieve these positions by virtue of appropriate qualifications and merit rather than political affiliations.
Just the other day, the Leader of the Opposition had the occasion to wonder whether in fact the Justices of the Peace, who had just been recently appointed were done in a manner that was impartial. Establishing a National Appointments Council, in my view, will go a long way to assuaging any fear that any of us or any member of the public may have about these appointments being political. The proposal is that the Council comprises three people appointed by the Queen’s Representative, on the collective recommendation of the Chief Justice or a Judge of the High Court
There is only one appointment recommended by the Political Review Commission that is not in this Bill and that is the position of the Speaker of Parliament. It is my view, and I am not sure that the Commission had the opportunity to hear that view that the appointment of the Speaker is and must remain the prerogative of Parliament.
MR N. GEORGE: Oh, man. Another silly thing. Absurd!
HON. SIR G.A. HENRY: The Report also recommended the establishment of a Leadership Code. So, a part of this Bill is seeking a constitutional opportunity for the Government, after the next election, if it so desired, to put in place an Act to establish a Leadership Code for all Members of Parliament, for all Ministers and for all Heads of Departments and others who hold top jobs in the Civil Service. I want to make it clear Mr Speaker, that when we vote on this Amendment we are not voting into existence a Leadership Code. All we are doing is amending the Constitution so that it provides the opportunity for an Act to be subsequently enacted to establish a Leadership Code. I believe, Mr Speaker, such a Leadership Code is in order and should this Government have the privilege of being the next Government, it will enact such a Code.
The Commission did not recommend it but it is being recommended to this Parliament to amend the Constitution in order to allow for the opportunity for an Act to be put in place to govern the position of Members in Parliament when it comes to the question of crossing the Floor. When we vote for this Bill, we are not voting against the right of any Member to cross the Floor on any issue. All we are voting for is an amendment to the Constitution to give the incoming Government the opportunity to consider putting in place the management of that process. The idea, Mr Speaker, is not to prohibit anyone from crossing the Floor on, say, conscience issues or other issues.
Should such an Act come into existence it must be very careful to recognise the right of the Member of Parliament to cross the Floor. At the same time, that Act must balance that right of the Member of Parliament against the right of those who put him into Parliament. Again, I want to make it clear Mr Speaker that we are not voting for that Act. We are simply voting for the opportunity, should the next Government so desire, to bring in place an Act that manages the process of a Member moving from one side of the Floor to another, on any given issue.
Thank you, Mr Speaker.

MR N. GEORGE: Mr Speaker, I stand to give the support of the New Alliance Party to this Amendment. I understand that this Amendment is being put through the Constitution to enable another Act to be created further down the line. I would be very cautious of such a bill being subsequently drafted. We have to realise and appreciate that Members of Parliament also need the protection of the law as they also have total unfettered constitutional rights. If and when an enabling Act is put through, I would like to say to this House that this Parliament should make sure that such an Act does not infringe on to the fundamental human rights, freedom of action of Members of this Parliament. Ironically Mr Speaker, when you look at the history of this country, and when you look at the history of this Parliament, many Governments have been formed as a result of Members of Parliament crossing the Floor, as a result of the actions of turncoats in Parliament. It happened to both sides, to both political parties in the past. Nobody can say that it only happened to so and so. There has been, I think, equal amounts of Floor crossings. If we had to mark the steps, I am sure it will balance up with those going from this side to that and that side to this.
It is understandable, Mr Speaker, that those with a high degree of honour, those with a high code of leadership standards detest turncoats. I would also like to see the new enabling legislation allow Members to express Motions of No Confidence, and to take part in Motions of No Confidence, and not be restricted to non-meaningful acts of conscience. I would not like the freedom of any member of this House to express a Vote of No Confidence in either his own Government or in anybody in this House.
