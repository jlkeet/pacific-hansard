Parliament. But all that has changed since yesterday, since I had a meeting with the Honourable Tom Marsters and the Prime Minister yesterday in the true spirit of discussion, compromise and consultation.
Now I want to tell you, Mr Speaker and the House, the reasons why we in the Overseas Constituency objected to this. Perhaps if I give you the views of Professor Hodge from the University of Auckland, it will convey to you the basis of that objection. This was done in the Talkback Show over Radio 531PI conducted by the host, the big fellow called Joe Ka. When asked what the faults and discrepancies were in this Amendment, this is what he said. First, he said that the principle of this is unique. He liked it. He liked the idea of the Prime Minister of a country picking somebody from outside of Cabinet. It’s a novel idea. This idea should not be thrown out but explored further, but as the Amendment is presented to Parliament, it had certain deficiencies. The first is that there are insufficient checks and balances within the present Bill and there ought to be introduced means by which Parliament would be given the right to approve or disapprove. The Commission of Political Review did recommend this that the Prime Minister can appoint somebody from outside of Cabinet, but it did also recommend that this be done with the approval of Parliament.
The second suggestion was that Parliament be given the right to remove that person through a vote of No Confidence should that person be found incompetent or unsuitable for the position at a later date. The idea of giving the Prime Minister the sole right - it doesn’t matter who the Prime Minister is, whether it is Sir Geoffrey Henry, whether it be Dr Terepai Maoate, whether it be the Honourable Norman George or whether it be the Honourable Tupou Faireka - it doesn’t matter. It is not the individual that matters but it is the position giving that person who holds the office of the Prime Minister the sole right to appoint somebody from outside of Parliament without the necessary checks and balances to make sure that person is suitable. On the strength of the comments of Professor Hodge, people called in objecting, voicing their objection that when the Member goes there he must oppose this. And before I left, I had consultations with the Executive and they confirmed their stance.
As I said, all this has changed since the meeting that we held yesterday. I know by being informed that the Honourable Member, Tom Marsters did voice his objection at the Second Reading of this Bill and so did the Members of the Democratic Alliance Party and again all that has changed since yesterday.
In that meeting that we had, we reached certain agreements.
