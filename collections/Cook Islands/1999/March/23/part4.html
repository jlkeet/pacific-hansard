Now, Mr Speaker, coming back to the International Partnership (Amendment) Bill. As I said it is a very short Bill. It has been on the Order Paper for some time now. It was tabled together with the Trustees (Amendment) Bill, which we passed about two or three weeks ago. Like that Bill, which is now an Act, this Amendment Bill is of a similar nature, to correct some of the omissions, some of the typographical errors which occurred in the 1984 Act, which is the principal Act. I specifically mention the year 1984, Mr Speaker, otherwise I would be accused of committing errors in Bills which in this case I wasn’t responsible for. Yes, Mr Speaker, the principal Act was

passed in 1984 and part of this Amendment Bill in front of us is to correct some of the mistakes that occurred in the principal Act. However, Mr Speaker, there are other additions and some other amendments to that Act which is before us. For example, you will find in Clause 2 that the definition of Trustee Company has been amended so that the new definition will include any wholly-owned subsidiary of a Trust Company and this extension also will make the new definition consistent with other Offshore legislation.
The more substantial change is an amendment to Section 55 of the principal Act whereby the word “general” is deleted. This allows for a Trustee Company to be a managing partner in an international partnership. I believe, Mr Speaker, this change is necessary because there are countries which incorporate this amendment in their legislation. For example, Mr Speaker, similar United States structure require managing partners and a section as amended will allow us to comply with these United States structures and thus open up a potential new market.
A further change, Mr Speaker, particularly to Clause 5, which reflects the deletion of the word “general” in Section 55. It removes the distinction between a ‘general’ and a ‘managing partner’.
Those are the major improvements in the principal Act and as I pointed out earlier other changes are merely typographical errors.
While we are talking about Trustee companies, Mr Chairman, I thought I would raise the issue of money laundering because when we debated the Trustee Company (Amendment) Bill, the Leader of the New Alliance raised the issue. He specifically mentioned an article in the Reader’s Digest. When I made inquiries of the issue with the Trustee Company’s principals on our island, I was informed that this article was written about ten years ago and since then a lot of legislation has been through this House to correct the accusation that was mentioned in the article - I haven’t read the article myself - and at the same time to improve the security of our offshore industry.
Secondly, I was informed it would be a rare case if money laundering comes through the Cook Islands because we only have five Trustee Companies on the island. At the same time we only have two Class “C” banks through which money-laundering activity could be put through and we only have two of those. While some Offshore jurisdictions have many, many Trustee Companies and many, many banks through which money-laundering could take place, our system is easier to police.
At the moment, Mr Speaker, I have in front of me a Report on money-laundering issues. The actual Report itself is called a 1998-99 Report on Money-Laundering Type Policies. Unfortunately, I haven’t had time to go through the Report itself, Mr Speaker, but reading the preamble it states that the Cook Islands is one of the safest places to do Offshore Banking business. Our legislation is such that it is very difficult for money-laundering to take place. The Offshore industry in our country knows very well that if anyone of them breaks the rule, they will automatically lose their license.
