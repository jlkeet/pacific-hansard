Cook Islands Natural Heritage Trust Bill, Second Reading.
The Honourable Deputy Prime Minister.
HON. I. AKARURU: Thank you Mr Speaker. I move:
That the Cook Islands Natural Heritage Trust Bill be now Read a Second time.
Mr Speaker speaking briefly to the principle and merits of the Bill the Natural Heritage project was established in 1990 to collect scientific and historical data on plants and animals of the Cook Islands, including traditional use of plants and animals and to make this information available to the general public and special interest groups. To date Mr Speaker, the project has collected and systematically filed a great deal of information and published a variety of posters and books for the public. The project has prepared several books on specific groups of plants and animals and on several natural heritage topics. A major ongoing task is the development of multi media database on all significant plants and animals. The database presently contains 3000 plants and animals and when finished will have 5000, having a basic description of Maori names. This project collects and produces information, which is available and useful to Government and non Government agencies involved in environment, history and cultural developments. This is what the Natural Heritage Project does Mr Speaker.
Since its inception the natural heritage project has operated from the Prime Minister’s Department, the Prime Minister’s Office, the Ministry of Works and environment and Physical Planning and the Environment agency, Taporoporo. In each case the

umbrella group provide salaries for its staff but little for actual production and operation. Furthermore the project has never been comfortable fit with any of these bodies. This is true even with the project under the environment services. The fit is not comfortable. The environment service operates under the Rarotonga Environment Act and is only concerned with Rarotonga whereas the project covers plants and animal life throughout the Cook Islands. Furthermore the collection of data concerns flora and fauna and is not the function of Taporoporo. Over the years the project has had difficulty negotiating with overseas agencies for funding because of its lack of an independent identity. The establishment of a natural heritage trust by providing the trust with a separate identity for external assistance for specific programmes makes available information on plants and animals of the Cook Islands.
Initially the project will need continued support from Government who ultimately we hope this will be supplemented by significant private contributions or by international support as well.
This Bill Mr speaker establishes a Natural Heritage Trust administered by trustees. The Minister for the Environment appoints one trustee who will be Chairman of the Board of Trustees. The Director of the Environment Services, the Financial Secretary and the Head of the Ministry of Education each appoint one trustee. One further trustee is appointed jointly by the respective heads of the Ministry of Agriculture and Marine Resources. There are also provisions for the appointment of trustees by donors who make significant contribution to the funding of its activities. Such trustees will hold office for the period of the expenditure of that same donation while the other trustees holds office for a period of three years.
The principal objective of the Trust is the gathering of scientific data and knowledge of nature in these islands. These include study and research on the traditional knowledge and practices of Cook Islanders in respect of flora and fauna of this country. A subsidiary objective is to make this information widely available.
Mr Speaker, to achieve its objectives the Trust has a number of powers. It may establish Museums, hold exhibitions, publish pamphlets and booklets and undertake other activities to educate the public about the animals and plants of the Cook Islands.
The Bill provides for the trustees to appoint a Director. The Director may appoint staff with the approval of the Board. The strict accounting requirements contained in the Ministry of Finance and Economic Management Act 1995-96 extend to the Trust. The Trust must also furnish its accounts and an annual reports to the Minister for the Environment. This Report, Mr Speaker, together with its accounts attached must in turn be laid before this Parliament.
Mr Speaker, the Bill concerned a small but successful project which has been in operation for nearly a decade and has the full support of the Minister for the Environment. The Bill aims to give the project a higher profile, an independent identity and widen its base of support both locally and internationally and, Mr speaker, I am confident this Bill will help achieve that object.

I was so proud when I heard that there were some birds that are rare but found in the Cook Islands. It was the first time Mr Speaker that I heard of these rare birds like the Kakerori on the island of Rarotonga, the Tangeao on the island of Mangaia, the Kuramo which is found in Aitutaki and a few islands in the Tahitian Group and the Kopeka on the island of Atiu.

And I also hear that on the islands of Rakahanga and Manihiki alone, we have the Kakaia a Maui. This is precluded to those two islands only, how wonderful. These are just a few of the birds that I could mention.
We still have the flora of the Cook Islands and this is something that this Natural Heritage Trust wants to be able to show the world. We know that there are people in the world today who want to give money for the development of the fauna and the flora. They can’t because the Cook Islands Natural Heritage Trust cannot be identified as a Trust, this is why we need this Bill. If I look at the ways that the Board of this Trust is set out I think it is well done. I’m happy that there is one person on this Trust Board that is appointed by the Secretary of the Education Department. Because in this person he will report from the Trustees to the Education Department and therefore we would be able to implement some of the plans that the Natural Heritage Trust would like to put into the Schools.
There is just one area that disappointed me and I spoke to our legal advisor. I see here that there are times when we will need the Natural Heritage Trust to turn the value of Avana into a reserve. But they are not been given that authority. We can see now that this is the responsibility of the Tuanga Taporoporo and I would like to see us give the Natural Heritage Trust the opportunity to be able to recommend the Tuanga Taporoporo to consider this option. The Tuanga Taporoporo has that authority but we need the Natural Heritage Trust to assist them by making such recommendations.
As we can see, all that this Bill is authorising is to give the Natural Heritage Trust the power to be able to go out, identify, catalogue and to find descriptions in books about these elements and eventually place these into a data base form. This Bill is not giving them the authority to protect our flora or our fauna. This is why I raised this matter, Mr Speaker, thank you very much.
