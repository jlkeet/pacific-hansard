Mr Speaker, this Bill in front of the House is a continuation of Government’s aim to update and modernise our legislation pertaining to the electoral process. It is also being presented in recognition, Mr Speaker, of certain expectations of our people. These expectations, Mr Speaker, have been identified by the Commission of Political Review in their Report on reforming the political system of the Cook Islands. This Bill adopts certain recommendations made by the Commission in their Report. The main part of the Bill focuses on one of the Commission’s findings, that is, that voters want to be able to recall their Member of Parliament if he or she performs unsatisfactorily. Some of us here, Mr Speaker, in this House today, need to be honest with ourselves and admit that just before the elections are held we are out there among our constituents, being seen to be hearing the concerns of our electorate and making promise after promise to them. Mr Speaker, after we have succeeded in obtaining office, I wonder how many of us here in this House today live up to the expectations we have generated amongst our people? How many of us regularly go back and consult our people on matters that affect them and then try to the best to get Government to address their concerns?
In Government today, Mr Speaker, Heads of Ministries are required to enter into a performance agreement with their respective Ministers to ensure that they carry out their respective responsibilities effectively. It can be said, Mr Speaker, that this Bill sets out the performance agreement of Members of Parliament with their constituents. Through the passage of this Bill, we will be stressing the point that we and all persons who succeed to an office in the future are fully accountable to our people for their full term in office. The importance of accountability is continued throughout the Bill by the introduction of provisions that impose on political parties and independent candidates, the responsibility of providing records or returns of all electoral expenditure to the Chief Electoral Officer. The Chief Electoral Officer has the authority to audit a return if he believes the return is erroneous in any particular way. It is further provided in this Bill that these returns shall be made available to the public for inspection. As referred to, in the Commission’s Report, Mr Speaker, we in Parliament and other political candidates need to be mindful of the pressures and demands on our supporters during the campaign period. It can be a burden on our people to supply food, transport and other resources during this period before the election and we need to be aware of the tensions campaigning brings to our families and the community as a whole. For this reason, Mr Speaker, there are further provisions in this Bill which provide for the control of all forms of electoral advertising or election activity. Provision, Mr Speaker, is also made for the limitation of the campaign period to a term of five weeks prior to the date of the election.
Other miscellaneous provisions in the Bill, which also follow the recommendations of the Commission for Political Review, include the requirement that all voting papers include a photograph of the candidate. The Bill also provides for the opportunity for any candidate, if he or she wishes, to provide a statement about the candidate, to be set out in the form provided by the Schedule in this Bill. For the sake of our people listening in to this broadcast, this statement includes background information on a candidate’s education, employment, community service, political experience, sporting

Finally, Mr Speaker, the Bill includes a provision which requires any person who is elected to the Island or Vaka Council or as Mayor, to resign from that position before accepting a nomination as candidate for any election to Parliament.
In explaining the purpose of this provision, Mr Speaker, it is my view that it is important to ensure that all candidates to be elected as Members of Parliament, begin campaigning on an equal footing.
