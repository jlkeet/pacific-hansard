I have a prepared statement here which I have elected to read to you. I would like the Member of Parliament for Overseas, who is also a resident of Aitutaki, where this former Honourable Member came from - the Prime Minister Dr Joseph Williams to make a statement after this statement. We will be observing two minutes silence thereafter and I am asking the Honourable Member for Amuri Ureia Paora Teiti to close our remembrance of this former Honourable Member with a prayer.
“A former Member of Parliament of the Cook Islands, from 1968 to 1972, Mr Ngaa Upu passed away in Melbourne on the 16th of October this year just 27 days before his 75th birthday. Papa Ngaa Upu was born in Aitutaki on the 12th November 1924 and educated there. He is survived by his wife Nellie and their 12 children. He married Nellie Teaukura in 1943.
Ngaa had a long and varied career in the Public Service. He trained as a schoolteacher and taught for many years before becoming a Community Development Officer for the Ministry of Internal Affairs. He also served as an Island Councillor. He was elected to Parliament, on the Cook Islands Party ticket, as a Member for Aitutaki (one of three MPs for Aitutaki at the time, along with Dr Joe Williams and the late Mr William Estall) and he served for one term, from 1968 to 1972. Throughout his life, Ngaa had many interests and talents. After leaving Parliament he retired to his home island where he played a prominent part in local affairs. He was a superb sportsman – being a master at the traditional sport of pua, cricket, rugby and represented Aitutaki in cricket and rugby on numerous occasions.

Ngaa was also a strong and faithful member of the Seventh Day Adventist Church – achieving the position of Head Elder – the equivalent of Tauturu Orometua in the CICC church, a position for which he was greatly respected by Church Members. In addition to all of the above he was also a baker and a planter. For many years, he was Chairman of the Aitutaki Growers Association”.
Papa Ngaa, who lived in Melbourne, Australia for the last seven years of his life, passed away while attending a service at his beloved church. He was a kind, patient and tolerant man who earned the respect and affection of all who knew him. His body was returned for burial on the island of his birth, Aitutaki. He will be greatly missed.
I now call on the Honourable Dr Joseph Williams, the Prime Minister and Member for the Overseas seat.
HON. DR J. WILLIAMS: Thank you Mr Speaker. Thank you for giving me the opportunity to pay a last tribute to Papa Nga Upu.
With your leave, Mr Speaker, I will reserve the traditional Parliamentary greetings until a later time in this sitting.
As you stated in the Obituary, Papa Ngaa Upu and I entered politics together in 1968. We stood together in the 1968 General Election with the late William Estall. We worked together for the benefit of our constituency and I cannot forget the teachings that he gave me while I was a young gun at that time.
When he shifted to Melbourne, Australia he became part of my constituency. He was a constituent of the Overseas Constituency and I am happy to speak on behalf of all those from the Overseas Constituency in paying tribute to the late Papa Nga Upu.
Two weeks before he died he came and saw me to discuss a certain matter for presentation to Government. This matter concerned the possibility of offering recognition for his services in the church, in the sports, in the community and in politics. We did not complete that discussion. Two weeks later he died. I told him at that time that when I come over to Australia we would continue the discussion.
Mr Speaker, Members of the House, it is my honour and my privilege to say these few words in remembrance of the late Papa Ngaa Upu. Thank you Mr Speaker.
