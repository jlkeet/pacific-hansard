Oral Question 9

HON. M. BROWN: Thank you Mr Speaker and thank you also to the Honourable Member of Matavera, the doctor for money on the Opposition side, for your question this morning.
The answer to your question will be open to you in our Supplementary Budget. I also point out in response to this question that we consider the prices of goods that are increasing. We are well aware that inflation is impacting on our price of goods and the price of imported goods in particular into our country.
Inflation into our country is important. To service our 165,000 tourist that visit our country which is close to 10 times our resident population. Local companies, local people import up to two $200 million worth of goods and services from New Zealand in particular. These includes food, frozen foods, dry goods, timber, building materials, vehicles, tools and machinery. All the things that help us live to a standard of living that our people now demand.

So, when the price of those things overseas goes up, the price goes up when we bring it into our country as well. And the way to beat inflation is to make more money and you spoke about the GDP. We have to keep our GDP moving up so we can afford to pay for these things that we bring into our country. But there are some things we can do to avoid being impacted by GDP and these are things that many of us are very good at but maybe some of us has been a bit too lazy to do it and that is to grow our own food.
Our locally produced produce, food, meat, chicken, fish are products that do not attract the impacts of inflation. So, one of the things to do is eat more local fruit instead of going and buying something from the shop. Plant taro, plant rukau, bananas, pawpaws and more, they produce all the time. Our forefathers knew this life. If we cannot afford to buy a can of corned beef, the pot will be filled with breadfruit. When we were kids, we would have eight breadfruit in a pot and only one corned beef. But it has changed, today it is the opposite. But these are little things that each household can do to help combat the impacts of inflation. Inflation is a reality for us. The biggest one was in fuel that we saw just a couple of years ago.
So, to answer the question from the Member from Matavera, our strategy is to grow the economy but not just to grow the economy but to also diversify the economy. Not also to diversify but to also bring in all of our outer islands into the economy. The over half a billion-dollar economy that we have today is largely generated on Rarotonga and to a lesser extent, Aitutaki. We need to include and increase the contributions from islands like Mangaia and Nga-Pu-Toru and the Northern Group. But to do that, we need to put the infrastructure in place on these islands so transportation link is important. Energy is important for power and access to labour or machinery on our islands is very important especially if we want to bring them in to be part of our economy. And that is how we look to grow our economy and help combat the impacts of inflation. I was going to say Climate Change Mr Speaker but do not get me sided on that we will be here for another half an hour. Thank you Mr Speaker.
