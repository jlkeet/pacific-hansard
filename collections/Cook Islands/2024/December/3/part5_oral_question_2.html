Oral Question 2

MR V. TUPA: Thank you, Mr Speaker. I have a Supplementary Question for the Minister of Justice on the question posed to him yesterday.
We have established an organisation known as the Religious Advisory Council. However, the issue I see with this organisation is that they lack a clear mandate for their establishment.
Concerning this organisation, I suggest that this is an opportune time for the government to address this matter by structuring the organisation and establishing a clear mandate. This would enable the Council to effectively consider issues related to religious activities and potentially grant it the authority to review applications from those wishing to establish church organisations.
In light of this, I would like to urge the government to address the matter by designating the Religious Advisory Council as the body responsible for reviewing applications, particularly from Christian Churches seeking to establish themselves here in the Cook Islands. I would specifically like to highlight the Revival Church and the Apostolic Church, both of which should be given due consideration. It is essential that this council reviews all such applications appropriately.
MR SPEAKER: I see the Honourable Minister Mokoroa.
HON. V. MOKOROA: Mr Speaker, if I can refer to the Prime Minister to answer this, then I will make my answer.
MR SPEAKER: Thank you. The Honourable Prime Minister; you have the Floor.
HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker, and thank you to the Member for Matavera for your request. I would like to refer to the 1975 Act that established the recognised Churches here in the Cook Islands. It has been 50 years since that legislation was enacted, and currently, I believe only four Churches are recognised under that Act. However, as we know, many more Churches have been established in our country since then, representing a variety of congregations and denominations that actively preach the Gospel within our nation.
Next year as we celebrate the 60th year of the establishment of our country as a modern country we know that we have changed and the changes have been significant, Mr Speaker not just from the size of our country which have moved from 250 square kilometres of islands to now two million square kilometres including oceans.
Furthermore, the population of our country 60 years ago was about 30,000 people, but today it has grown to well over 100,000. Our society has become more diverse, with many Cook Islanders now having intermarried with individuals from other cultures, so we have Cook Islanders who are also part of other cultures.
This diversity invites not only a variety of cultures but also an array of religions. As the Member stated, I believe it is indeed timely for us to review our current Act and assess whether changes are necessary. However, we must also be mindful of our responsibilities, as highlighted by the Leader of the Opposition and others. We are signatories to human rights agreements, including the Bill

One example I would like to share to illustrate how our country has evolved over the past 60 years, Mr Speaker, is this year, I was advised that the last horse in the Cook Islands has died. There are no more horses in the Cook Islands, Mr Speaker; the final one has passed away. This brings to mind the stories shared by Papa Dan Kamana, who lived into his 90s and often recounted that in his youth, owning a horse was a sign of being a man.
We once had hundreds of horses in our country, Mr Speaker, which were essential for work purposes, but they also served as a means of transportation, even taking people to dances in Titikaveka.
