Oral Question 15

HON. M. BROWN: Thank you, Mr Speaker and thank you to the Leader of the Opposition for your question. The community policing initiative, Mr Speaker, aims to involve our community in enhancing the police presence in our neighbourhoods. We have received feedback from the community expressing a desire for a dedicated office where police can be stationed. Members in

Takitumu are already working on identifying suitable locations for police operations, and I know similar efforts are underway in Puaikura as well.
In the past, there was a police office at the Aroa Hall, and this is a sentiment echoed by our communities. They desire not just for police to drive around, but to be visibly present and engaged within the community. We are working closely, Mr Speaker, with the police to address their financial needs, ensuring that we can implement this initiative effectively in the coming year.
However, the reality is that addressing juvenile delinquency requires more than just police involvement. Many of these children, particularly young boys, need programs that will engage them and direct them towards a positive path, preventing them from becoming juvenile delinquents. In the past, we have seen success with programs that utilise sports to engage our youth, helping them to become productive members of the community. We must build on these successful initiatives to provide our young people with the support and opportunities they need.
When we examine our current scholarship recipients pursuing advanced studies, we see that the majority are young girls. This raises the question of whether we need to create more opportunities to engage young boys who may be missing out. It is important to involve all sectors of our community to guide and support the development of our youth.
Schools, Church groups, sporting organisations, and various community groups all play vital roles in guiding our youth. However, the most significant influence comes from parents. I'm sure, Mr Speaker, you remember a time when the police officer was a figure of authority in the community, and children were afraid of them. But they were even more afraid of that Mama with a kikau broom, a jandal, or a branch from the hedge ready to discipline them. That kind of discipline kept many children in line. Today, the dynamic seems to have changed; we are more scared in hurting our children, and many children are no longer scared of anything.
In terms of the juvenile case under the Ministry of Justice, I know the Ministry of Justice has been active in ensuring the re-establishment of the Juvenile Crimes Prevention Committee. And of course, today measures a taking to use restorative of justices as a means of identifying these kids that are causing problems and making sure that, yes, they do face the consequences of any crimes that they may commit but do it in a way that they appreciate the impact that it had on their victims. Thank you, Mr Speaker.
Today, efforts are being made to implement restorative justice measures as a means of addressing issues involving troubled youth. These approaches ensure that while children face consequences for any crimes they commit, they also come to understand the impact of their actions on their victims. Thank you, Mr Speaker.
