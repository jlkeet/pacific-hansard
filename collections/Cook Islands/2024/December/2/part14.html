The aim of these reviews, Mr Speaker, is to evaluate our country's ability to cooperate effectively with established standards. Our initial peer reviews took place in 2017, and the Cook Islands received a very positive rating for its capacity to exchange information. In light of the subsequent growth and improvements in both the quality and quantity of information exchanges, as well as enhancements to the standards themselves, a second round of assessment was initiated just last year.
This latest round includes a legal framework assessment and peer reviews that also cover technical, operational, and information security aspects. During this process, several gaps in our legal framework were identified, and the Global Forum provided recommendations aimed at helping us maintain our positive rating. Our country is required to address these recommendations by implementing the necessary legislative amendments by 31st December of this year. This is essential to qualify for another round of onsite assessments and reviews in 2025.
To provide our Members and the public with a better understanding of this review, the security of information is very important and the security of tax information in particular is of significant importance to the Global Forum.
Mr Speaker, some of the areas identified for improvement extend beyond legislative requirements, it is also in the basic operations. For example, all doors in the RMD office that hold tax information must have security codes. The staff that work there must have proper identification cards with ID cards to swipe and allow access to these rooms.
It is a big change from how our public service has operated for many years and maybe we do not see the actual need for this level of security. However, the Global Forum has its standards to maintain and we are obligated to maintain those standards, so we must follow suit.
Not only that but now there's also a requirement for proper due diligence to be conducted on employees or people who will work inside these departments.
It is these sorts of requirements, Mr Speaker that compels us in our Government Agencies, many of them now to change the way we do things and to be mindful of increased security measures that are being imposed on our country.
It was just a few weeks ago, Mr Speaker, that there was a significant attempt to hack into the government’s central network. However, thanks to the robust firewall security systems we have in place, we were able to fend off these attempts to hack our system from overseas. However, a couple of our local companies in the hospitality industry, their systems were compromised, Mr Speaker and we were able to provide support to reduce any damage caused by these cyber security threats.
The financial transactions reporting requirements that our country have signed up to, Mr Speaker, is an example of the increasing collaboration among international jurisdictions to share information. Additionally, the need to protect the integrity of our financial centres and enhance our cybersecurity measures will only intensify as the world increasingly moves toward digital currencies.

Mr Speaker, I would like to highlight a couple of key aspects of this Bill. Firstly, it will eliminate the technical requirements for identifying beneficial owners, which are referred to as the ultimate principle. These requirements will be incorporated into the Financial Transactions Reporting Regulations. These requirements are outlined in the Schedule of the Bill.
Additionally, as previously mentioned, the Bill will align the definition of beneficial ownership in the Companies Act 2017 with the definition in the Financial Transactions Reporting Act as amended in this Bill.
Moreover, the Bill will grant the Commission the authority to direct certain individuals to retain a bank record in situations where that bank is being wound up.
The Schedule of the Bill introduces changes to the Financial Transactions Reporting Regulations, which include definitions of "control." This encompasses any individual who controls, whether acting directly or indirectly, and either individually or jointly. Furthermore, the definition of control also includes any person occupying a senior managerial position when the beneficial owner cannot be clearly identified.
In essence, Mr Speaker, the purpose is to ensure accountability within the company, specifically among senior management, if the company is structured to conceal the true identity of the beneficial owner. Additionally, the Bill includes provisions that exempt professional service providers, such as lawyers, advisors, and trust companies, from being classified as beneficial owners of a legal entity.
As you can see, Mr Speaker, the amendments involve a great deal of fine-tuning to ensure their relevance in today’s context. The consultations surrounding this Bill with the finance sector have demonstrated strong support for aligning our practices with international standards, which is crucial for achieving positive outcomes in next year's review. It is my honour, Mr Speaker, to support this Bill in the House, as I am confident it will significantly contribute to our positive results. Furthermore, it is essential for maintaining the competitiveness of our financial sector on a global scale and ensuring its continued contribution to our economy in the future. Thank you very much.
