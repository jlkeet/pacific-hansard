HON. M. BROWN: Kia Orana to all this afternoon.

the people to Mangaia, that same number could have been accommodated with 18 flights from the Saab. Which highlights the importance of a significant key piece of infrastructure if we want to lift the standard of living in our Pa Enua islands. And this is the rationale, Mr Speaker, behind the amount of money that we are looking at to seal firstly, the airport in Manihiki.
As the Minister of Foreign Affairs indicated, there is no economic formula that would justify an expense of that amount, there is no financial formula that would convince somebody, some economist, that this would be a worthwhile investment. That’s because, Mr Speaker, they only look at one thing and that is the bottom line and they look at it from a very narrow finance and banking term perspective. We know the investment in a project like that, Mr Speaker, the return and the payback period is in much longer terms.
It took that long, Mr Speaker, for those economies to build to take advantage of this investment in infrastructure. To today we now have an economy that generates 600 million dollars a year. That of course is generated from the economies of Rarotonga and also from Aitutaki – very small contributions from the rest of our islands and it will remain small, unless and until we make the investment that enables them to join our economy. Therefore, this investment in Manihiki airport is not just a political promise, it's a reality that we have to put in place.
We have to understand that investment of this nature, any infrastructure investment is very, very expensive for a country with a small economy like ours.
We recently completed the 10-year Te Mato Vai Project at a cost of close to a hundred million dollars. That was the biggest infrastructure investment that we have made since the project to open our international airport in 1975. Yesterday, a question was asked about the sanitation project for Takitumu. That’s another project, Mr Speaker, that will cost in excess of a hundred million dollars. Our country simply does not have that sort of money sitting around yet and, because of our graduation to high-income status, the only avenue of access available to us is through borrowing.
We have to be innovative, Mr Speaker. We have to go out, we have to look at options where we can find financing for key critical infrastructure but also financing at the same time to build resilience against the impacts of climate change. Our voice has been very loud at the International forums at the Asian Development Bank and other International forums that we have attended over the years and as a result of our advocacy, Mr Speaker I am happy to announce to us that the Asian Development Bank just late last year and in February confirmed this year, a new financing instrument available to countries like us that currently who offer terms of financing at 20 years, now will offer terms of financing for up to 40 years at one percent interest with a ten year grace period.

Just four years ago in 2020, Mr Speaker, our GDP was $600 million, a record for us and it dropped by 30 percent due to COVID and only today, four years later are we back at that same spot we were in 2020 and while our earnings and purchasing power decreased over time, the cost of goods, the cost of food increased over that time. So, we are in a period of catch up, Mr Speaker.
The question was asked just earlier about why we are not engaging our key private sectors in certain projects and as much as possible the government policy is to engage our private sector and even more so if it is a competition between our private sector and an international company, we will defer for our private sector to carry out the work. There are times however, Mr Speaker, if the private sector cost exceeds what we have available, then we have to look at different ways of trying to deliver the same project and that is what we are doing right now with the Manihiki project. This is what we call a public sector model, if you like or the public works model, the old way of doing things where the project is led by the Ministry but engages private sector as needed and that way, we can manage to keep the cost at a level that we can afford to deliver this project.
Manihiki will be the template for how we will carry out this work, and we will look to roll out the sealing of the runways right around our islands but the northern group is the priority because that is the most expensive place to fly to at $1,500 - $1,700 for a one-way fare. Because when the fares drop and the cargo capacity increases, we will start to see a little economy starting to develop on these islands. We have seen it in Aitutaki. We have starting to see it in Atiu with the small developments of accommodation there.
I know that if we have the airfares dropped to at least even half for flying up to the north many of our own people will want to go and visit their islands in the northern group if there is a regular service and if you can go at an affordable price. Then you will see someone opening up a little shop. We will see people sending products to the market. Then you will see small economies taking shape and it does not happen overnight. It takes time Mr Speaker as we have seen the example of both Rarotonga and Aitutaki.

Recently, Mr Speaker, I just returned back from a country in the Caribbean called Antiqa and Babuda two islands. This country depends heavily on tourism like us. They have a GDP of $1.6 billion. The advantage that they have is that they are very close to the source market of the United States who has a population of 260 million people. We live in a part of the world that is quite isolated and source markets are much less than what America has. But despite these differences and the challenges that we have, I still firmly believe Mr Speaker that our country already in the region leads the way.
And I firmly believe Mr Speaker that with the investments that we have made over the years and the investments we see in this year’s budget and moving forward we are setting our country on a pathway that will provide the prosperity and the standards of living that our people deserve. And I speak not just of the tourism potential that we have but also of our mineral resource which I have not spoken about at this session. But already in the area of seabed our country is seen as one of the world leaders.
World leaders in the sense of ensuring the protection of our ocean. World leaders in the sense of our approach to exploration being of the higher standards of integrity and transparency. We are now gathering such a wide body of data and information from our exploration that Pacific leaders has agreed at the last Forum for the establishment of a Pacific Centre of Excellence on Deep Ocean Science to be based here in Rarotonga.
This is knowledge and information and data Mr Speaker that we own, the country owns. It’s knowledge about our ocean that we own and its knowledge that we should protect. There is a value to this knowledge and information Mr Speaker and it is important that we understand that but it is also important that we let the rest of the world know that this knowledge is ours but we are willing to share it.
Next week I will be receiving a report on the valuation of the mineral resource that we have in our EEZ as a result of the exploration that has been conducted to date. Next month we will begin public consultations on the development of a Sovereign Wealth Fund to ensure the protection revenues that our country may receive from our mineral resource. This is a fund that is being established to ensure the responsible management of resources but also the disbursement of revenues to help build our country up. It is important we have a discussion with our people to let them know what we are looking at and also to hear their views on what they think revenues that we earn in the future from mineral resource where they should think it should be going to.
Therefore, Mr Speaker, I am very happy with this Appropriation Bill that is before the House and I look forward to continuing our discussion with the Members of Parliament on the appropriations across our Ministries. I would like to really acknowledge my team at Ministry of Finance, the
