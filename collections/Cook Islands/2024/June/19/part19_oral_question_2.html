MR V. TUPA: Mr Speaker, while there is no notable difference in the increase for this Vote Item, I would like to voice my support for the budget allocated towards Audit.
Mr Speaker, I am aware that a Bill is currently being reviewed to strengthen our Audit Office. I strongly feel that this is a Vote Item that requires our attention, and I anxiously wait for the Bill to be presented before this House. Once implemented, I believe that this Bill will significantly alleviate the workload and responsibilities of the Head of Audit and their staff.
Furthermore, I am convinced that this Bill will not only provide assistance to Ministries and Departments that subscribe to this Honourable House, but also empower them to adhere to their respective Reports. While the Audit Office currently has the ability to address Ministries who have failed to fulfill their Reports, there is a dire need to strengthen the process of requesting their compliance. I am confident that this Bill will ensure that the Heads of Ministries will comply with their budgets without hesitation.
Mr Speaker, I would like to highlight an important aspect to consider when reviewing the financial reports provided to us by the Audit Office and PAC. In the Mangaian dialect, a report deemed "QUALIFIED" denotes a satisfactory report. Conversely, a "MODIFIED" report suggests that there are issues with the work. Lastly, a "DISCLAIMED" report signals a severe problem. As such, I am in full support of this Bill and eagerly anticipate its presentation to this House at the earliest opportunity.
Thirdly, Mr Speaker, I would like to take a moment to acknowledge the contributions of Desmond Wildin. Desmond has been an integral part of the PAC committee from the time of my joining in 2018 until my resignation. His intelligence and work ethic are quite remarkable. Fortunately, he was a member of the team during my tenure on the committee. However, I understand that his current employment contract is set to expire in November of this year.
I would like to make a formal request to the Minister responsible for Audit, and I urge that my statement be recorded in Hansard. My humble request to the Honourable Minister is to consider re-engaging Desmond for another term with the Audit Office. In addition to this, I propose that he be given the opportunity to train another individual who can take on his role. Given the demanding nature of his job, I believe this arrangement would ensure that the Audit Office will continue to function effectively. Therefore, Mr Speaker, in the event that the relevant Minister is listening, I implore them to consider my request and take suitable action accordingly.
To conclude, I support this budget. Thank you.

HON. T. PUPUKE BROWNE: Thank you, Mr Speaker. I would like to take this opportunity to share my thoughts on the allocation at hand. As part of our visit to the New Zealand Parliament in Wellington, we had the privilege of meeting with the Auditor General of New Zealand. Personally, the meeting has enlightened me on the significance of the Audit Office.
Despite the fact that our Director of Audit was appointed as an advisor for the Public Accounts Committee, I was not fully aware of the magnitude of this Office until our encounter with the Auditor General of New Zealand in Wellington.
My understanding is that the Audit Office should operate with independence, which implies that in the event it becomes independent, the allocation for this Office will not be part of the Budget presented to us today. An independent Audit Office would only be accountable to the Speaker of Parliament. The appointment of the Director for Audit would no longer be made by a Minister but rather by the Speaker. Furthermore, if the Director of Audit were to be removed from their position, only the Speaker of Parliament would have the authority to do so, not a Minister.
It was explained to us that the rationale behind such operations is to provide independence to the Audit Office. This way, during their investigations, the Office would not have any hesitation to present their findings and recommendations to the government. As humans, we may hesitate to show everything during investigations that involve our bosses as it may lead to unfavourable consequences.
What I discovered next was that the New Zealand Audit Office is aiding our Audit Office and their legal representative stationed in Wellington is currently working on drafting laws that will be passed on to our Office to help us realise this goal. Given this, Mr Speaker, I firmly support this initiative.
In my view, two Ministries that should operate independently are the Audit Office and the Office of the Ombudsman. I believe that if we enforce legislation to establish complete independence for the Audit Office, we can eliminate concerns that people may have regarding the efficiency of the Audit Office in carrying out its duties.
Despite there being minimal adjustments made to the allocation for this Office, I support this Vote Item. Moreover, I urge the Minister responsible for this Ministry to expedite the process as we are aware that it often takes significant time to draft legislation for Parliament's consideration.
During our meeting with the Auditor General in New Zealand, he assured us that he would assist to the best of his ability. Additionally, I had the opportunity to attend a PASAI meeting, which comprises the Auditors of countries within our region. Fortunately, we have an excellent

HON. V. MOKOROA: Thank you, Mr Speaker. Thank you to all colleagues for your thoughts on this Vote Item.
Mr Speaker, we all know that the main function of the Cook Islands Audit Office is to provide an independent assurance to this very House, the Parliament, and the public on the government's accountability and stewardship of public resources. They conduct financial audits, performance audits, and compliance audits. We have heard their involvement in assisting our PAC members to provide some insights, some guidance to our PAC members as to who is complying and to who is abusing these very appropriations that we are discussing in this House.
Mr Speaker, we have heard the compliments and acknowledgement from both the Leader of the Opposition and the Members that the Audit has contributed to them. Since taking up the Ministership for Audit, I have never been on any of Audit’s meetings and I am very happy to hear that the Leader of the Opposition has been to two Audit conferences and meetings which makes me very very proud.
It is also good to sit from the government side and hear that the Opposition is the main people that are promoting and praising the Audit Office rather than the Minister himself. So, to reiterate Papa Vai Peua’s statement, “tei mua rai te Kuki Airani.”
Mr Speaker, this office is operating at 68 percent staff occupancy so out of 22 staff, there is only 15 currently employed now. And this office has completed auditing all of 2019 Cook Islands Government financial statements. So, the main challenge for this office now is to complete three years’ worth of audit from 2020 to 2022 and to get it done by the end of 2026.
This office is required to carry out Annual Audits for 59 individual government entities, 14 Outer Island Administrations, 15 State Owned Enterprise, seven Ministerial support office, 13 Crown and Statutory entities. I have been assured by the Audit Office that they will continue to work with MFEM to complete three years’ worth of audit.
I am very grateful and thankful for all the Members of Parliament that have supported the increase and the increment required for this particular office. We all know that the Audit Office plays a crucial role in promoting good governance and helping improve the effectiveness and efficiency of government operations in the Cook Islands.
With those few comments, thank you very much, Mr Speaker.
