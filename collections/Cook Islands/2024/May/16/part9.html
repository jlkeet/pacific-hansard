HON. T. ELIKANA: Thank you, Mr Speaker. Mr Speaker, we have brought this Bill before the House to rectify an oversight in our current immigration legislation, as I am sure we are all aware. This oversight was identified as a result of the actions taken by the Friends of Fiji, challenging the provisions of the Cook Islands Immigration Regulations of 2022. These provisions offered preferential treatment to our friends from New Zealand over other countries. I am confident that we all know the decision of the court on this matter.
The key finding of the court was that these preferential treatments were invalid because they are not part of the domestic or national laws of the Cook Islands. It is important to note that regulations are executive decisions that typically follow parliamentary actions, such as legislation or principal acts. However, the court found that the law passed by Parliament does not allow for this preferential treatment.
The oversight occurred because Section 3 of the current Immigration Act does not refer to international obligations, and the court was clear that the joint declaration between the Cook Islands and New Zealand is indeed a treaty. If the Cook Islands intends to include the provisions of this treaty and joint declarations as part of our domestic laws, they must be incorporated into an Act of Parliament.
This was not captured in Section 3 of the current Act, which I mentioned in my opening statement. However, the proposed Amendment Bill now includes this in Section 3. In the past, there have been several instances where Cook Islands' international obligations were incorporated into domestic law, such as in Section 2 of the Civil Aviation Act of 2002, stating that the purpose of this Act is to:
(a)Establish rules of operation and divisions of responsibility within the civil aviation system
in order to promote aviation safety and security; and
(b)Ensure that the Cook Island’s obligations under international aviation agreements are
implemented.
The convention on international civil aviation, ratified by the Cook Islands through the Cook Islands instrument of adherence, effective September 1986, is central to the Act. As I mentioned earlier, the joint declaration between New Zealand and the Cook Islands allows for preferential treatment, especially in Article 2.2, where it states that "the government of the Cook Islands will accord New Zealand citizens preferential considerations in respect of entry into and residence in the Cook Islands."

Unfortunately, our current Act does not provide for this preferential treatment. However, as is evident from the proposed amendment to Section 4 stating that:
Section 3 replaced (Purpose) Replace section 3 with: Purpose “3 The purpose of this Act is to manage immigration to the Cook Islands in a way that- balances the national interest, as determined by the Crown, and the rights of “(a) individuals; and
“(b) meets the international obligations of the Cook Islands.”
The thrust of this proposed legislation is to rectify the oversight identified in the court's decision. Thank you, Mr Speaker.

HON. T. PUPUKE BROWNE: Mr Speaker, I rise to express my views concerning this Bill. Firstly, I have perused the Chief Justice Keene's judgment from 7 March 2024, relating to the case fought by the Friends of Fiji. Moreover, I was a member of the Select Committee on Immigration at the time, and the Crown Law Office also advised the Committee.
Regarding the Minister's mention of the oversight, it was undoubtedly an oversight not to inform the Select Committee about the joint declaration with New Zealand. The previous Minister who spoke was also a member of the Select Committee. Therefore, three lawyers in the Committee did not identify it.
However, I am confident that if we had been informed of the joint declaration with New Zealand, we would not be discussing this Amendment Bill today. Conversely, I believe that the amended Section 3 addresses that issue because it makes reference to us meeting our international obligations.
During the legal proceedings, the court faced three questions and resolved the first two. The final question relates to Article 64 of the Constitution, which addresses fundamental human rights and freedoms.
