HON. T. PUPUKE BROWNE: Mr Speaker, I wish to share my thoughts on this Bill. Its objectives are commendable – to reduce the prevalence of diseases in the Cook Islands and promote better community involvement for delivering improved health.
We are all aware of the state of health in our country – possibly the highest rate of obesity in the Pacific, if not in the world. It is disheartening to see individuals, on bikes or otherwise, struggling with their weight. This Bill, which seeks to repeal the 2004 Public Health Bill, offers a chance to help our population avoid preventable diseases like diabetes and high blood pressure.
As a parliamentarian and a human being, I understand the importance of personal responsibility for our well-being. This Bill should provide a turning point, leading to greater sensitivity to health concerns. The advertisements by the Health Ministry ought to be advertised more often to bring awareness to the public.
The second objective of the Bill, community involvement, is just as critical. Using myself as an example, if I delivered a message concerning the risks of these illnesses, it may be received as better when shared by a delegation from the Ministry of Health. It is essential that our island administrators and Island Councils partner to educate our people because ultimately, it is us who are responsible for our own wellbeing.
Over-indulging in sugar, oil, and flour can cause a range of these health issues. Import restrictions on sugar and fizzy drinks have been suggested, but this option may not be feasible. However, we must find ways to tackle these challenges gradually and teach our people about good health.
The $1 million allocated in the budget for referrals confirms that our health is deteriorating; hence, the need for us to practice good health for a healthier population and save costs in the process. The Cook Islands Christian Church attempted plain water consumption at their gatherings without sweets, but it lasted only a month. So, it is crucial to incorporate churches and traditional leaders in the planning process as they play a vital role in the community.
In conclusion, we must own our health and not rely only on the Ministry of Health, and I support this Bill. The objectives are admirable, and it is necessary to apply them equally effectively.
