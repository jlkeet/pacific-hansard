I would like to express my thoughts on this Bill, specifically regarding smoking. I recall a couple of years ago, when Honourable Member George Angene and I were the only Members of Parliament who smoked at the time. During one parliamentary session, we both opposed a Bill proposing a 75 percent increase in the cost of cigarettes. Our united front was successful in rejecting the proposed cost increase.
Today, my friend has switched his stance on this Bill. As a result, I see no point in opposing it as I stand alone. I would like to appeal to the Honourable Members to bear with me, as many of my friends are heavy smokers. Despite this, I have chosen to support my friend, the Honorable George Angene and stand in favour of this Bill.
Another reason why I am in support of this Bill is that I have a personal connection to the dangers of smoking and vaping. Let me be clear, I am not making any promises that I will quit smoking myself - that would be a lie. However, about two years ago, a child in Mangaia was diagnosed with a severe health condition and was flown to New Zealand for treatment. Upon examination, doctors discovered that one side of the child's brain was significantly damaged. After further investigation, it was revealed that the child, only three years old, had been smoking vape. It remains unknown how the child obtained the instrument, but it is believed that it may have been brought to the island by a visitor.
This tragic incident highlights the dangers of vaping and smoking, and I share this story with you all to raise awareness on this pressing issue. Moreover, I support this Bill precisely because this child is a member of my family, and I am committed to preventing similar incidents from happening to anyone else. I firmly hold the belief that smoking poses a serious threat to the health and wellness of our people. Whether it be tobacco, marijuana, or vaping, these products can all have detrimental effects on our well-being.
Furthermore, this Bill directly addresses the issue of smoking, particularly in regards to drivers of vehicles. Those caught smoking inside a vehicle will be held accountable under this law, as such behavior poses a risk to the health of passengers and those in close proximity.
For these reasons and many others, I am happy to lend my support to this Bill. I understand that my colleague and I might be the only smokers present in this House and have already expressed our views. Therefore, there may be no additional speakers on this topic. Nevertheless, I remain committed to supporting this crucial issue. Thank you, Mr Speaker.

Mr Speaker, the age of a person should not be a factor in the discussion of tobacco legislation as exemplified by the case of my grandfather, Papa George Faimau Robati, who is 94 years old and still smokes Rothmans. I argue that smoking is a matter of freedom of choice and should not be regulated by law. One's personal choices such as what to eat or drink should not be interfered with by others' opinions. Our party opposes this bill because it punishes those who choose to purchase and smoke cigarettes despite knowing the risks.
As one of the oppositions in the Cabinet, I hold firmly to our belief that each individual should be responsible for their own smoking habits. We are against the bill that is presented in this House because it interferes with personal choices. We do not believe that the government should be concerned with preventing illnesses caused by smoking as this would be interfering with personal choices. If the aim is to protect people, then why not penalise people who put on weight? This bill is not just for smokers but targets only a specific population, which is unfair. To penalise shops and importers who sell tobacco products by imposing a $10,000 fine is too drastic and disproportionate. Businesses contribute significantly to our economy and they should not be penalised for selling tobacco products.
I agree that some people choose to give up smoking, and the public is informed about the health risks associated with smoking. However, we are not against the spirit of this bill, but we cannot support the punishment of our people and their ability to purchase their own cigarettes. We need to protect our people, but we cannot do so by punishing them. In fact, smoking is not the only issue as marijuana smoking is widespread on Rarotonga, and what are we doing about that?
I feel that this bill is unjust as it affects the livelihood of our people. We need to create a balance between the cost of cigarettes compared to other daily essentials, such as a tin of corned beef at $8 and cigarettes at $65. One solution is to increase the cost of other items such as corned beef. My company promotes planting local crops like taro and maniota, which are available at Prime Foods and Wigmore. We should be supporting our local people by increasing the cost of necessary items rather than penalising them for buying cigarettes. Thank you, Mr Speaker.
