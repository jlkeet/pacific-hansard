The Last Budget
Mr Speaker, I would like to reflect on our last budget from 2023, which was themed “Nurturing aNation.” That budget was all about support. We increased payments for caregivers and for our most destitute citizens, supported key positions within Health and Education, and we took care of our environment. The Nurturing extended to supporting our role in the region – as Chair in the Pacific Islands Forum, we also took steps to strengthen our Blue Pacific continent and advocate for our regional neighbours around the world.
Mr Speaker, I want to highlight the significant steps that we have made on the regional stage, particularly in our Blue Pacific continent. The Cook Islands has led the way in progressing several key initiatives, including the Pacific Resilience Fund (PRF), and the Pacific Partnerships for Prosperity (PPfP).
The PPfP is a critical development. It is designed to help Pacific nations bolster their defenses against climate change and extreme weather events. This fund represents a unique opportunity for our development partners to contribute in a way that empowers us to manage our own climate response effectively.

I am very proud to lead in particular the establishment of the Pacific Centre of Excellence on deep ocean science also known as PCDOS which will be based here in the Cook Islands and positions us to become a preeminent ocean science hub in the region and the world. Economic outlook
Mr Speaker, today’s budget shows that despite some economic headwinds that we have experienced, the economic outlook for the Cook Islands is positive, as we have climbed out of the depths of the pandemic and are moving rapidly back towards the prosperity that we once experienced.
Our GDP is on the rise again, very quickly as we fill in the gap from the hole caused by the pandemic – our growth rates will return to more levels as our cycle continues and GDP is projected to grow by just over 4 percent in the financial year 2025. This is solid growth when compared to any other economy but it does pale in comparison to the 14 percent that we will experience this year and the 27 percent growth from 2023.
We must also acknowledge, Mr Speaker the surge in price inflation over the past two years. This peaked in late 2022 and was the highest inflation that we have faced in our history. Thankfully the rapid growth of prices has slowed down but prices still remain high and they are unlikely to fall back to where they were just over two years ago. Factors beyond our control such as the global supply chain disruptions, geopolitical tensions and various conflicts resulting in volatile oil prices and supply distributions have been the major contributors to this inflation.
Over the past year to March 2024, inflation was 4.2 percent and food prices rose by 4.8 percent. I would like to stress that this inflation is something that government cannot control as it is coming from outside of our country but this slowing of inflation is welcome, even if it is not slowing as quickly as we would like.
The slowing down of inflation is welcome with its peak in 2022 being well behind us. This is consistent with the experiences in New Zealand and Australia who have seen inflation declining though not as rapidly as we would desire but government is continuing to undertake work throughout economic recovery roadmap to ease costs and put downward pressure on prices.
Mr Speaker, we are also actively working on advancements that promise to transform the way we do business in our country. Our collaboration with the banking sector aims to enhance our payment system. This is crucial as it will empower our local businesses to explore and expand into eCommerce and to use digital platforms which are vital in today’s global market.
Furthermore, we have undertaken significant efforts to setup regulations for our utilities. The goal here is straight-forward, we want these sectors to operate more efficiently and ensure that our people pay only what is necessary to maintain these essential services.

Fiscal outlook
As we look at this year’s Budget, it reflects our ongoing commitment to fiscal responsibility. This budget moves towards a neutral fiscal stance, meaning we aim to spend only what we bring in, avoiding any unnecessary debt. This careful balance is critical as it prepares us to handle future economic shocks or natural disasters without relying excessively on borrowing.
Mr Speaker, managing our nation’s finances responsibly means ensuring we have enough funds to handle unexpected challenges such as, being able to respond to another economic shock or natural disaster or that we have the fiscal capacity to draw down on debt to respond if we need to and as we have done in the past.
