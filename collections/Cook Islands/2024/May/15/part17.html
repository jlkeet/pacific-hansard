KEY BUDGET INITIATIVES – OPERATING
Salary banding for Teachers
Let me speak to some of our key budget initiatives Mr Speaker, investing in our tamariki or children is critical for our future and to do this effectively, we need to invest in the teachers who guide them. We understand the important role that our teachers have in guiding the next generation and giving them the skills to succeed in life – for all of us. The former United Nations Secretary General, Kofi Annan once remarked that “education is the premise of progress in every society” and that is true of us here in the Cook Islands, so we are making investments in education – to continue that progress.
Therefore, Mr Speaker, in this Budget, we have allocated $5.2 million over the next four years to bring our teachers up to pay scales which more accurately reflect the important work they

Providing support for critical healthcare:
Each year, many of our people face serious health challenges, meaning travel overseas for treatment – either from the Pa Enua to Rarotonga or to New Zealand. This Budget increases our commitment by providing an additional $1 million per year to ensure those who need treatment can access it. This builds Mr Speaker on last year’s decision to facilitate access to private healthcare in New Zealand, improving health outcomes for our people.
Non-Government Organisations
Mr Speaker, many NGOs in our community operate alongside Government to provide important services to our people. Unfortunately, as a result of our success in being classified as a high- income country, some of these organisations have found it difficult to access funding from development partners as that funding is now being withdrawn. So we now have to step in and fill in the gap. With the critical work that these organisations do, especially in helping our most vulnerable people, government is providing almost $700 thousand in funding to continue support for this sector which includes NGOs such as the Cook Islands Family Welfare Association, Te Kainga, Punanga Tauturu and the Cook Islands Civil Society organisation – all of whom contribute critical work to our society. This will allow these organisations to continue to support our families and our children to have the best possible opportunities in life, as well as provide some help and guidance for those most in need.
This funding initiative was initially in the last Budget, for one year to allow the organisations some time to be able to seek funding from development partners. However, this funding has so far proven difficult to source, therefore the government needs to step in and provide funding.
Tourism and the economy
Tourism, Mr Speaker remains a vital part of our economy, driving two-thirds of our economic activity.
Over the past, government has made some significant investments in the sector especially with regard with the airline connectivity, and we have seen those investments begin to pay off. Government is continuing to support these investments in additional flights and additional capacity to bring people to the Cook Islands.

Mr Speaker, the flights are a key component of our tourism sector, however they do not achieve much if the people do not know much about them. So, government is providing additional funding of $4.7 million over four years for marketing the Cook Islands as a destination to help support the investment in connectivity to fill these planes up. Further to this, $1.8 million in funding for Destination Development which will help to provide an environment for tourists when they visit – including in the Pa Enua.
Pa Enua transport
Mr Speaker, many people have seen the discussions in the media around key transport and in particular shipping links to the Pa Enua. I am proud to report that this Budget include a considerable investment in shipping in the Pa Enua to ensure regular freight connections for our people outside of Rarotonga and Aitutaki. In this regard we continue to work closely with the governments of both Japan and China to ensure fit for purpose vessels that can be secured to support our Pa Enua development.
This work will help make life on our Pa Enua a little easier, and reduce one of the barriers to their economic development – assisting in the entrepreneurial spirit of our people in the Pa Enua to run businesses and create investment outside of the two hubs of Rarotonga and Aitutaki.
Building Up Reserves
Mr Speaker, we remain committed to building our financial reserves. This includes a sensible plan for repaying the loans that were taken out during the pandemic and moving towards fiscal surpluses. We are also ensuring that funds are available in our stabilisation account and our loan repayment funds to meet future obligations.
Capital Investments
Mr Speaker, let me turn to our capital investments. I have spoken many times about the importance of capital expenditure, about its ability to employ workers and stimulate our economy, particularly while investment from private companies is limited. Governments all over the world know this, and the Cook Islands is no different. That is why it’s one of the Economic Recovery Roadmap work streams.
Capital expenditure in this Budget will continue the government’s investment themes from the previous year, and in particular make use of the Infrastructure Trust Fund, which has been generously provided by New Zealand.

In this Budget, government has focused on projects that are either already in progress or in development in order to complete existing projects with importance to the Cook Islands. These are designed to employ local contractors and labour, such as the Te Tau Papa O Avarua – the Avarua Town Plan project which is funded under the Infrastructure Trust Fund. This project, and indeed many in the infrastructure space, are geared toward ensuring both that we build the infrastructure that we need, but also that there is activity for local construction firms, engineers and trades peoples – as without that pipeline of work, the skilled labour involved in this sector will probably leave. The government is continually in discussion with development partners around financing of work in this sector to ensure that there is work ongoing – especially while our private sector repair their balance sheets. As these discussions bear fruit, announcements will be made, but rest assured, we keep a very open dialogue with our partners.
Physical Infrastructure
Let me turn to physical infrastructure Mr Speaker. Infrastructure Cook Islands will continue important work on physical infrastructure on Rarotonga and the Pa Enua, with an ongoing focus on bridges and roads on Rarotonga, and marine and air infrastructure works in the Pa Enua, including on Nassau and Manihiki.
The work on Manihiki airport in particular will pilot a new approach to infrastructure development in the Pa Enua. We know that the cost of building thins in the outer islands is prohibitively high in many respects, so to deliver key projects, a ‘public works’ style model is being undertaken. This project will be used to shown how such a model can work and learn from it to be able to implement it in infrastructure projects for the future – keeping them as affordable as possible.
Cook Islands Investment Corporation continues its focus on building and repairing government buildings. All of these projects employ local businesses and labourers on both Rarotonga and the Pa Enua. And as well as spending through the Budget, the government is also actively discussing capital investment proposals with our donor partners. We continue to work with New Zealand on proposals to fund projects through the Infrastructure Trust Fund with a strong emphasis on shovel ready projects that will keep our people employed and help to retain our skilled labour.
Changes to social payments to help the most vulnerable
Mr Speaker, turning finally to our social support programmes.
I previously commented that inflation has been significant, but out of the Government’s control and while we continue to work on reducing inflation, we must also help our vulnerable populations more directly and as signalled in last year’s budget we have increased payment rates for the destitute payment, the power subsidy and the caregiver payment and consistent with our plans

from last year, from 1 July this year the Caregiver payment will go up again from $300 per month to $400 a month to better support those who care for others.
These payments, Mr Speaker help some of our most vulnerable people and those looking after our most vulnerable. Increasing these rates helps to bring them along with the recovery in our economy.
As well as these payment increases, we have seen more recipients of our pension and some other payments. There are more people turning sixty, Mr Speaker and to meet the needs of these increase numbers of pensioners we have allocated over $3 million over the next four years to cover these increased costs.
Minimum Wage Increase to $9.50 per hour
As we have seen announced by our Minister of Internal Affairs, the government has also confirmed an increase in the minimum wage from $9 to $9.50 per hour and for a worker on 35 hours per week, that’s an additional $900 a year. The increase of the minimum wage to $9.50 an hour has required an additional provision for government spending on personnel, primarily in the Pa Enua. The total cost to government is an additional 1.3 million dollars over the next four years.
Mr Speaker, today’s budget is about perseverance and ensuring that we are well placed to take advantage of the opportunity on the horizon which results from our strong economic management.
Therefore, I leave you with a Budget that balances fiscal responsibility and the needs of our Nation as we continue our journey towards our prosperous future. Thank you very much for your attention and support as we work together to build a stronger, more resilient Cook Islands. I lay this Bill before the House, Mr Speaker.
