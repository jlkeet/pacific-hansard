Just a little history concerning Copyright. It began as a moral concept that eventually found its way into the law books. In Ancient Greece and Rome the copying of someone else’s work was condemned as morally wrong and studies of Roman literature show that the Romans were aware that the publication of a work involved a moral right to the work.
After the invention of the printing press in the 15th century, the copying of written works became easier and piracy began. The clergy looked to the kings of the day, over whom they generally had strong influence, to protect their interests in their libraries and their monopoly on many kinds of printed works, such as the Bible. From this resulted a number of royal decrees and statutes granting privileges. Most of these privileges were aimed at protecting what were then called stationers, who are today called publishers. Often, this protection was granted for commercial reasons to protect their investment in printing equipment. Now, let us ask ourselves what about the authors of those books? The growth of Parliamentary democracy led to a new era in law making and Copyright became no exception to this as the value of the printed word and the ability to reproduce it increased. Laws which were the forerunners of today’s Copyright laws were entered onto the statute books and these were passed in Germany, France, England, Prussia and so forth. The Constitution of the United States gave the Congress the power to vote, promote the progress of science and useful arts by securing, for limited times, to authors and inventors, the exclusive right to their inventions and discoveries. From the late 1700s until this century, some 200 years later, the Principles of Copyright law have remained basically the same. The principal changes have been due to changes in technology. As international travel and communications developed, countries began trying to keep pace with this. Some countries recognised each others’ laws by clauses in their own laws. Some entered into bilateral treaties, whereby they agreed to protect the inadequate, some, multi- lateral treaties whereby a number of countries all agreed to recognise and protect the products of each of them, were entered into. The earliest of these was in the 1880’s but the first major and truly international one did not come into being until 1952 when the Universal Copyright Convention was adopted. Now, coming to the Cook Islands, Mr Speaker, our country through New Zealand, is a member of this convention and the Berne Convention, for the protection of literary and artistic works. Under these conventions, the Cook Islands is obligated to make provisions for the protection of literary and artistic works and to provide for the adequate and effective protection of the rights of authors and other Copyright proprietors in literary, scientific and artistic, cinematographic works and paintings, engravings and sculptures. Under section 627 of our Cook Islands Act 1915, we adopted New Zealand’s Copyright Act 1962. It has often been said that there was no Copyright law in the Cook Islands up to the one that we are about to put in place, but that is not right because we have this in the Cook Islands Act 1915. There was provision for it. There has been some confusion over whether this Act was in fact adopted correctly and this had led to the public believing that we have no Copyright laws in the Cook Islands and there is a need to clarify this through the introduction of this Copyright Bill. Now, this Bill before this Honourable House promotes the Principles contained in the Copyright Act 1962 and further recognises the advancement of new technology. This Bill has been long awaited by the members of the public and I am sure that it addresses the problems that they have in protecting their creative interests, not only here, but in other countries as well.
