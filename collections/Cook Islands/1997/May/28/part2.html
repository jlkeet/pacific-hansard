However, Mr Speaker, as the Committee went through the task of examining submissions and going through clauses in the Bill, I became convinced that the Bill is the best that we could devise for our country. Although based on tax regimes from other countries, the Committee set out as much as possible with the intention of introducing a local “face” to the Bill. By adapting the Bill to the needs and conditions pertaining to the situation of the Cook Islands, it will create a more sound and effective Bill of our own, instead of unnecessarily just adopting overseas provisions and measures which may in some areas be irrelevant. For example, I think at the last meeting we held we introduced two measures we thought will help our local business people, our local farmers and fishermen. The two issues took us a long time to deliberate over, but eventually, and finally, we decided that there was a need to encourage the local fishermen and farmers. Those measures that were introduced saw the zero rating of food stock and fertilisers. This means there will no longer be any taxes due, for food stock and fertilisers.
At this stage, Mr Speaker, I would like to correct some misconceptions. I do not know whose fault it is but it appears that the perception of the general public is that VAT (Value Added Tax), is going to be imposed on top of the TOT (Turnover Tax) that is currently in existence. Let the public be assured that once the VAT and the new Income Tax is implemented, Turnover Tax will be repealed. In other words, the Value Added Tax will replace the Turnover Tax, and not be combined with the Turnover Tax. As a result, there is very little change as far as tax payments are concerned. For example, at the present time when you buy something from a shop, you will be paying something like 11.1% on top of the price of the item you bought to cater for Turnover Tax. Once this new package is introduced you will pay 12.5% instead of 11.1%. The difference, Mr Speaker, is only Rarotonga will pay that. In the Outer Islands they have been paying over 16% Turnover Tax. I think that is something that has been misunderstood in the past. I do not think that most people have been aware that the Outer Islands are paying more Turnover Tax than the people

HON. P. POKINO: Mr Speaker, I would like to thank the Mover and the Seconder of the Motion and also the Members of the House for agreeing that I be able to continue to speak.
As I was saying, Mr Speaker, the Outer Islands pay Turnover Tax on the cost at the door of a business or retailer. In other words, they buy their goods in Rarotonga, pay for the transport of those goods to the wharf, pay for the freight on a boat, a barge or plane, then again the transport of the goods into their shops and addition of the mark- up and finally 10% on top of all of that. Which at the end is equivalent to about 16 something percent.
What this VAT is proposing is to do away with the VAT on freight which includes the boating transport of goods, so that the outer island people pay 12.5% of the cost of the goods from Rarotonga. Plus of course, in addition, the mark-up of the business people in the Outer Islands.
As you can see, this package is fairer to the Outer Islands and unfair to Rarotonga by a difference of 1.4%. Therefore, the reduction to certain expenses will allow a reduction of the cost of the whole package. Lesser expenses required of them I assume, then what was expected to be paid previously. In fact, Mr Speaker, the response and the vibration we have received from the business sector is that- once the VAT is in place, the costs of the actual goods will be reduced as well. That is just one part of the VAT package that I would like to see in place, although there will be other advantages. For example, the plane fares to the Outer Islands will not be subjected to VAT. And as we are aware, the people using the planes are mainly Outer Islanders, so that is an added advantage as far as the Outer Islands is concerned.
