Mr. Chairman, I have already indicated to the Minister in charge of the Bill that the Opposition would like to see a speedy transition of the Bill as there is nothing new to cover, and therefore we could expect the Bill to be completed by this afternoon. There are however two queries, maybe while we are going through the clauses the Minister might be able to get hold of the advisers because these two matters irk me somewhat. I was on the Select Committee and these issues were discussed by that Select Committee, but I am still puzzled about these two areas. I want to mention these two areas quickly and resume my seat and hopefully get a reply to it later on in the discussion on the Bill.
The first one is to re-examine the question of pig food. When we were on the Select Committee we were advised by the Government experts on the Bill that pig growers are already paying Turnover Tax on pig food, and because of that there is no point in giving pig food exemption under the Value Added Tax. That was the advice given to us.
I have since that time held discussions with pig food importers, and they say while it must have been on paper somewhere that Turnover Tax ought to be charged on pig food, the practice for at least the last five years is that Turnover Tax has not been charged. I think the officials were correct when they said that on paper Turnover Tax was chargeable on pig food, but in practice it was not being collected.
A major importer the Honourable Minister Tupou Faireka confirmed he has not been charged Turnover Tax on pig food he has been bringing in. Therefore that confirms it to me that while the officials were correct in saying pig food is subjected to Turnover Tax, it has not been charged - they were not collecting the tax on it. I would like to draw this to the Ministers attention because following on from that precedent it is clear to me that pig food has not been exempted in the Second Schedule. Although, according to the Second Schedule- animal feed, not being dog food or cat food, has not been exempted. I do not know what that means. It also applies to imports on or before the 30th of June 2003. Perhaps the Minister can clarify this issue. I am quite

We spent a lot of time in the Select Committee explaining this to the officials who consisted of the Solicitor- General. He knows how we feel about it. This was a unanimous feeling amongst Committee members, that before somebody appears in Court, knowing that if you face a charge under any tax law these are very tough and harsh. You do not escape. Now it would upset me greatly if, having listened to the Committee - and I put a lot of onus on the Solicitor-General - he knows our position on this clause and we maintain that we are legislating for the Cook Islands - that we cannot be very harsh compared to, say, like large cosmopolitan countries like New Zealand and Australia, that we want our laws to have a human face. It would create some upset when the Committee says look, put some provision in there to allow mediation, to allow a last minute possibility of avoiding a charge, of diverting the full impact of the law against individuals and everybody agreed with this and then you wait for the Bill to come through and show that, I can’t see that anywhere unless I’m going blind and somebody can point it out to me. HON. P. POKINO: Thank you, Mr Chairman. Greetings to you, Mr Chairman, and Greetings to all Honourable Members in the House and Greetings to our people listening in to our debate this afternoon.
Firstly, Mr Chairman, I would like to thank the Leader of the Opposition for their assurance that the VAT Bill will pass through the House today. Secondly, Mr Chairman, I think some of his queries need some response from myself.
In response to his first query in regards to the exemption of VAT on pig food, I would like to state categorically that that is the case. As he has rightly pointed out in the Schedule, goods imported on or before the 30th June 2002 consisting of animal feed within Section 23.09 of the Cook Islands Customs Tariff, it is defined in there what animal feed is all about.
HON. N. GEORGE: Does it include pigs?
HON. P. POKINO: It defines stock food which means pig food, chicken pellets and other necessary food required to feed livestock. But it does not include foodstuff for dogs and cats.
Secondly, Mr Chairman, the exemption is also included for fertilisers to encourage our backyard farmers. Because they come under the same category as people who raise chickens or stock, these two measures the Committee felt should be included, a design to encourage the backyard farmers to fend for themselves.
The reason there is a definite date stipulated for the removal of this exemption is to encourage the farmer to take advantage of it straight away, rather than wait until they

are ready. But that does not mean to say that in the year 2002, if the need is still there, it will not be continued. As that is the date stipulated for the removal of the exemption. This is designed to provide a five year grace period for farmers to get their act together and take advantage of the concession incorporated in this year.
And secondly, Mr Chairman, with regards to the mediator service that has been spoken about, towards the end of the Committee’s deliberations while it was preparing it’s draft report for the House, the Committee made several proposals to be included in the report. The idea being for these proposals to be incorporated in this Bill. However when the proposals were sent to the officials, they felt that some of those proposals could be incorporated in the Bill, while others could become laws by way of Government Policy Statements. And unfortunately the mediator service came under that category. I think that in the original draft there was a mediator service provision under the RMD, the Revenue Ministry Department.
For the sake of the Members I have already instructed the Tax team to provide policy measures so that they could be introduced to coincide with the date of the implementation of the VAT Bill. That is not the only area where policy decisions need to be made. There are other areas the officials felt were more practical under policy issues, rather than be incorporated in the Bill.
The feeling was that once this is incorporated in the Bill, you are locked into what that Bill says. And there is very little flexibility to allow movement within the framework of what the intention is. I will take heed also, Mr Chairman, of the comments that the Bill can always be changed if the need is there. In fact, Mr Chairman, during the camera session last Thursday some concerns were expressed by some of the Members. But the Members were comforted by the knowledge that the Bill can always be amended to suit the time. So I would like to give further assurance that that will happen if there is a need to alter the Bill as we go along. Thank you Mr Chairman.
