This means, the RMD considers this Company in this way: if you are a business company in the country trying to make revenue, then the law of the country or the Tax Revenue Act is applicable to your company. Especially, Mr Speaker, when the government provides a subsidy to Air New Zealand and in that subsidy there is a provision for a profit by the Company. It is only fair that that profit pay tax like all other companies pay taxes to the country.
The RMD did collect tax from Air New Zealand but Air New Zealand disputed this in court. The argument being that under international airline conventions, countries do not tax each other airlines. It is a reciprocal arrangement where countries will not tax the airlines that operates in their country and in response, those countries will not tax your airline that operates in their countries.
So, as airlines operates in different countries around the world, they generally pay their tax in their home country. But because we do not have an international airline, this benefit only goes one way.
The matter really came up when we started to subside Air New Zealand’s flights into our country with the component of profit that was part of the subsidy. So, this profit is calculated and paid into the New Zealand tax collector.
The outcome of the court’s decision was that they supported the application by Air New Zealand; that Air New Zealand should not pay their tax due here in the Cook Islands regardless of the profit that we provide them, but that they should pay their tax in New Zealand.
And the end result is, Mr Speaker that the tax department, our RMD here as they have done with other companies and other businesses are required now to make a refund back to Air New Zealand, because it is a tax matter. There are matters of confidentiality involved but needless to say that matter has now been resolved in the courts and the RMD have been required to provide the refund to Air New Zealand.
Thank you Mr Speaker.
