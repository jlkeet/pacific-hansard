HON. M. BROWN: Thank you Mr Speaker. This Bill has been a result of some discussion and also the result of our referendum that we held in the last election and that is to allow for use of medicinal cannabis to help alleviate certain pains and certain illnesses that people have had.
It became apparent, Mr Speaker that people were using medicinal cannabis to help alleviate arthritis pain, to help alleviate in some cases asthma illness for breathing but it was all being done in contravention of the current law and legislation which prohibits the use of any cannabis product. Government was aware that medicinal cannabis is one of the fastest growing industries around the world with many countries adopting it as a form of therapy and a form of medicine.
Therefore, when we held the election, we also called for a referendum and asked the question to our people. Should we change our laws to allow medicinal cannabis to be used and to allow for research? Sixty two percent of our people supported the referendum, Mr Speaker. That’s more than triple the number that voted for the Opposition. But it is also more than the number that voted for the government.
So, I can say that, yes our people certainly gave a very clear message that, yes, they wanted this changed and to be applied and it is up to the government to see as to how we can adopt it.
The first step, Mr Speaker is to amend the Narcotics and Misuse of Drugs Act; it was to amend the Act, Narcotics and Misuse of Drugs Act. There is a list of prohibited substances that are listed in this Act, and cannabis is one of them in all of its forms.
Therefore, by providing an exemption, this Bill provides the exemption for cannabis products to be removed from that particular list of prohibited substances. This will allow therefore, Mr Speaker the importation of prescribed medicinal cannabis products to be able to be brought into the country. It will allow individuals to bring in prescribed medicinal cannabis provided it is accompanied by evidence that it was prescribed by an overseas medical practitioner.
The amendment will also allow providers of medicines here, pharmacies, the hospital and so forth to be able to bring in medicinal cannabis for them to prescribe domestically as well.
We also identified in the list of prohibited substances that there are some of our people who are returning home with terminal cases and in many of these cases they are bringing back or they are wanting to bring back specific pain relief which is on the band substances list.
So, we have also taken the opportunity within this amendment to allow certain opiate medicines that are used for pain relief or for end-of-life care, to allow them to use it for their personal use as part of their palliate care.

Once this Bill is passed Mr Speaker, there will be further amendments that will be required under the Narcotics Regulations and also the Ministry of Health, pharmacy and Therapeutic products Regulations. So, as this Bill will remove the cannabis as part of the prohibited substances, it will then fall under the Therapeutic Regulations, under the Ministry of Health, where it is treated as a health product rather than as a prohibited narcotic.
One of the concerns that we have identified and we do not want that to be reflected here is in other countries where they have allowed the use of medicinal cannabis, the regulations around its use have been so prescriptive that it has escalated and push the price of medicinal cannabis to be unaffordable.
So, we have identified some of these pitfalls and we will ensure that when we do put the regulations in place that it doesn’t make the cost of therapeutic or medicinal cannabis unaffordable for our people. So, I have heard that some of the ways that this product can be used is to be boiled up as a tea and drunk as tea. Others where the extract has been taken out as an oil to be applied either inhaling the oil or massage onto affected areas.
But it is important, Mr Speaker that our people understand that when we talk about cannabis there are many different forms of cannabis. I think there is a lot of stigmas associated with the narcotics side of cannabis which has the THC component, which is the component that provides the ‘high’ if you like, for users.
If you don’t understand THC, you can google and find out. It is TETRAHYDROCANNABINOL. This is very different from the other product that the medicinal side of cannabis, which is the CBD. This is the one that is used more for the therapeutic and medicinal side, which does not provide you with the narcotic high but it does provide you with the medicinal aspect of pain relief and other useful effects for curing. As I said, anything from arthritis pain through to relaxation of breathing, your breathing channels to allow a much more comfortable sleep.
We must distinguish also from the other cannabis product which is hemp. And hemp of course is a very useful product where the fibres that are used in the manufacturing of clothing and the seeds and flowers are used in health products. One of the outcomes of our Committee that was established to look at these amendments to the Bill was chaired by the Honourable Member for Pukapuka, Tingika Elikana. He was an appointed chair because of any personnel experience with cannabis by the way.
But purely from the legal perspective that we needed some good expert thinking. One of the things that came out of our study was to increase the amount of public awareness and education to our people. That while there may be a sense of fear or concern about using cannabis as medicinal product, we must make sure that our people are aware that there is a difference in these cannabis products. Some of them are useful for health reasons and of course some of it can be harmful if it is misused.
So, I look forward Mr Speaker to the rolling out of our medicinal cannabis industry, our regime and welcome those people who will be prescribed as medication to help them improve their healthy living. Thank you Mr Speaker.
