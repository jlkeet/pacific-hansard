ORDERS OF THE DAY
Interrupted debate on the Appropriation Bill 2023 - Consideration in Detail, Day 6
We are still on the Vote Item for Foreign Affairs, which we will be concluding soon. I call the Honourable Prime Minister; you have the Floor.
HON. M. BROWN: Thank you again Mr Speaker and thank you to the previous speakers on this particular Vote Item and I take note of the concerns that were raised by previous speakers in particular with regard to our foreign workers and also with the new Regulations that are now being applied.
The Members of the Select Committee of the Foreign Affairs Bill that circulated a number of years back now, I am sure they will be able to confirm, but the sentiment of our people at the time with regard especially foreign workers was that we needed to ensure we had good strong Regulations in place to control the amount of workers and the type of people coming into our country.
From what we recall of the feedback from the Select Committee Members, there seem to be a fear from some of our people, it may not be a rational fear that our country might be overrun with foreigners.
Our Act and therefore our subsequent Regulations in this regard seem to take this into account but also we had to take into account the needs of our labour market and the need for importing labour into our country.
There was a comment that our Permit Fees are nothing more than a money-making machine because the fees had been increased. This in fact is false and if we look at it, the work permit fees have been restructured so that you pay a $960 work permit which is a permit for three years. So, on a yearly basis, this is $320 whereas the previous work permit was $500 per annum.

I know when this was explained to many of our employers, particularly the large employers who bring in many foreign workers, they were very happy with this change in the fee structure which meant, they paid less.
The Regulations also add a level of flexibility into those who are bringing in foreign labour and this flexibility, Mr Speaker, large employers register themselves as an employer and the process for bringing in labour is greatly reduced.
But there is of course still the need for those checks that need to be supplied for medical checks, for police record checks and numerous others that every foreign worker coming into our country need to supply and this of course is to protect our employers in bringing their people and also to protect aspects of our society.
The question was raised about the application of bonds. The Regulation clearly states that the Principal Immigration Officer, at their discretion, may apply a bond of up to $10,000. This again is put in place as a protective measure only to be applied when one assesses the risk of certain individuals who may be coming into the country. These risks, Mr Speaker, may be associated with the health risk of a person bringing in certain health issues. It could be a character risk with a certain foreigner, maybe with gang affiliations that may be wanting to come into our country or employment risks associated with creating disruption within our country. That is why it is a discretionary application Mr Speaker, which is the responsibility of the Principal Immigration Officer to assess whether a bond should apply but whatever it is, that bond may not exceed $10 thousand.
A number of our foreign workers are currently under the old Regulations whereas the new Regulations now apply so there is a process where the Immigration Office will work with the employers to try and transition the conditions on their employers that they can be under the new Regulations.
Nearly all of the previous bonds that were imposed by the Immigration Department have been refunded back particularly during the period of the COVID restrictions when we were looking at ways to support our employers. Those that had bonds held by the Immigration Ministry have had their bonds returned and to date, no bonds have been imposed on any workers.
So, why do we have this regulation that allows bonds in the first place? Mr Speaker, the reason is so that people are aware and if it is applied, it is clearly stated in the regulations. Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Immigration does have the ability to impose a bond if required, which brings me to the issue of sponsorship because a foreigner can have a local sponsor to guarantee their ability to be able to live within their means here in the country without adding burden to our tax payers. Not all persons will require sponsorship Mr Speaker, if foreigners can demonstrate that

they have enough money to cover cost of living, their expenses, then sponsorship should not need to apply.
But certainly, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs officials are happy to work with any employer to help transition or migrate their current work force, their foreign labour force under the new conditions of the new regulations.
Is there a room for review of these regulations? Of course, yes, there is room. But when some of the suggested reforms come to making it free for permits to be issued, well that is a cause for consideration.
In regards to foreign workers using the Cook Islands as a gateway to ship or move to New Zealand, I know this has been an issue with a number of employers who have gone through the trouble of recruiting, doing the due diligence and in bringing foreign workers to work in their businesses and after just a few months or maybe six months, they resign and move to New Zealand. There is nothing the Government can do to stop people leaving. We are not in the business of owning people, they have the free right to be able to end a contract and most of the contracts that we have, either the employer can end a contract under certain conditions or the employee can terminate their contract.
I know this is an issue not just for us here in the Cook Islands but also a big issue in other pacific island countries, those that are involved with the Australian and New Zealand seasonal worker scheme. But we are a country that lives by the law of the land and therefore people must be free to travel, and to leave if they wish.
The reality is there is significant labour movement around our region and labour mobility around our region is encouraged under regional agreements that many of our countries have signed up to such as the PACER Plus Agreement. So, the question is really, how do we incentivise foreign workers to choose to work here rather than choose to pack up and go to New Zealand or Australia?
That is a difficult one to answer because Mr Speaker, for many decades, our own people have chosen to move to Australia and New Zealand in search of employment opportunity. The figures are indicating that our migrant workers, numbers are increasing and there are migrant workers who are now reapplying to come back to work in the Cook Islands. With those words Mr Speaker, I think the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Immigration deserve to be commended; this is a small organisation by comparison to many others in the region. The small team at the Foreign Affairs side of things, I know work is very, very hard in this week with the arrival of the Secretary General of the Forum and his delegation. They are working around the clock to ensure that their engagements with them are maximized.
