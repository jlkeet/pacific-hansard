Oral Question 3

HON. M. BROWN: Thank you Mr Speaker and thank you to the Leader of the Opposition in regards to the recession that was announced in New Zealand.
Economists in New Zealand actually refer to this as a technical recession and as the Leader of the Opposition correctly pointed out, a recession is declared when you have two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. Therefore, the December quarter in New Zealand was 0.7 percent negative and the March quarter was 0.1 percent negative; just crossing the threshold for the definition of a recession. It would have been difficult to see how New Zealand could have been in a recession other than the fact that in January, there was significant flooding in Auckland and in February of course, cyclone Gabrielle, which was the biggest natural disaster that New Zealand has incurred.
So despite these two major events at the beginning of the year, Mr Speaker, the New Zealand economy contracted by only 0.1 percent. In addition, New Zealand’s unemployment figure is 3.4 percent, a historical low for New Zealand, which means that the majority of the working age population is significantly employed.
Therefore, the question is what impact does this have on our own economy here. We have seen impacts in the last year or so as mentioned by some of our Members on the impacts of inflation but the March quarter of New Zealand shows inflation reducing, lower than expected and we have seen fuel prices also reducing and some of that we are seeing the early signs of that occurring also in our country.
In the preparation of our Budget Mr Speaker, the Ministry of Finance also does modelling on what the expected impacts are if we had a lower than the expected number of tourist arrivals coming into the country. The number of scenarios that we would prepare for in the event that tourism numbers were plus or minus five or 10 percent of what we expect.
In addition, our Tourism Ministry also does its work in part to make sure that we can mitigate against any potential impacts of reductions of tourist. Of course, to combat impacts of recession in our tourist markets in New Zealand, one of the key answers has been to diversify our tourism market, which we have seen recently with the arrival of flights coming out of Hawaii.

I think our tourism forward numbers gives us confidence that we can overcome any impact that this declaration of a recession has in New Zealand. Since the opening of our borders, our GDP levels have grown significantly in double-digit figures to where we are today. So, two different and very different scenarios and I hope that answers the question raised by the Leader of the Opposition.
