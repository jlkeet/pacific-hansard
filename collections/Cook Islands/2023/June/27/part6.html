HON. M. BROWN:Thank you Mr Speaker. Just to maybe clarify the Honourable Members’ question regarding this allocation and what can the Government do to help.
The $400,000 is allocated for a feasibility study to allow us to determine what is the best way that we can address this issue of coastal erosion that is taking place more and more. And because we know Mr Speaker that the protection of our foreshore is something that has to be done very carefully to ensure that we protect the environment. And while $400,000 might seems like a lot this is the amount of money required just to conduct the study as to how we can protect our foreshore.

And it is through this study, Mr Speaker that we will determine whether is it a concrete wall that’s the best thing, is it a rock wall, is it sand bags, is it more natural barriers that is required to help protect the foreshore.
One thing for sure, one of the significant causes of this coastal erosion, not just here on Rarotonga but also all of our islands and all around the Pacific, is the impacts of climate change. The one thing we know about the impacts of climate change is the incremental, it happens every year, and if the world does not do anything to reduce carbon emissions, this will continue to cause a big challenge for all countries, not just here in the Pacific but everywhere in the world.
But whatever the solution is, Mr Speaker I know that it will cost in the millions and millions of dollars to help protect our foreshore. That is why it is important our voice is heard when we go to International forums to make sure that countries who are causing the carbon emissions also take responsibility for providing funding to support countries like us who are feeling the impacts of these carbon emissions. While the establishment of the Green Climate Fund with the commitment of a hundred billion dollars a year to be made available to countries like us has yet to be realized so we must do what we can for ourselves in the meantime to help these households and these businesses that are struggling.
But this was really brought home to me yesterday at a function that was held at the Edgewater Resort which I have not been to for a while when I saw the foreshore has a big concrete wall now along the foreshore. Before they had this concrete wall, they had a rock wall that was there and before they had the rock wall, it was like a rocky beach going down to the sand.
So, it shows over time the impacts of what climate change has caused some places. When they see their beach being eroded, they had to put the rocks up that has been eroded, and they now had to put the concrete walls. What is the next step?
I think for us here, we have to do what we can and work together to make sure that we can at least slow down the impacts of erosion that is occurring on all of our islands. We need to put our heads together and come up with what is the best short-term solution while we look at addressing the long-term cause of this climate change. Thank you Mr Speaker.

HON. M. BROWN: Thank you Mr Speaker. Thank you very much to the Member of Matavera. There are two questions in there. The first one is asking about how Board members appointed onto the Boards? And the second one is in relation to the Mana Uira o Araura Board (Aitutaki Power Board). In terms of the Board appointments, the Government and Investment Corporations a number of years ago amended the legislation to allow people to put their names forward for consideration as Board members on all of our SOE Boards. This has allowed the Board of the Investment Corporation to have a wider pool of good qualified people to serve as Board members on our SOE Boards. And I have to say that the performance and calibre of our Boards now across all of our SOE’s are much higher than they have been in previous years.
For the Mana Uira o Araura Board in Aitutaki, this was established a number of years ago. Previously, the power in Aitutaki was run as pretty much as most of the outer islands are run and administered by the Island Administration. But we could see that Aitutaki’s power use and the economy was changing and growing with increased use of power and much more commercial application of power than it was seen in the rest of the outer islands. But it was not till the application of the solar project to Aitutaki to the value of significant billions of dollars of assets that we have invested in providing power for Aitutaki that it was decided the best way to be able to accommodate and manage this increased asset value was to create a Board for the Aitutaki power supply. That is why this Board was established and made up of members of the community from Aitutaki because not only were they now responsible for significant millions of dollars of assets but also in Stage 2.
