HON. M. BROWN: Thank you Mr Speaker. I would like to remind the House that on 1st January 2020, we graduated under the OECD as a High Income Country. It is not us that wanted to be on this level but it is the OECD that did so. It is them that sets the developed state of our country. Under their observation, they will analyse the GDP per capita. If they find we are above a certain threshold of GDP, we would be graduated from a Developing Nation status to a Developed Nation status. Developed Nations status means that we cannot have access to money from any Aid or Grant funds from any organisations or foreign nations. The only funding we can have access to is by way of loans.
Unfortunately, on 1st January 2020, we graduated to a Developed Nation especially under the tourism sector. But came the month of March 2020, there was the outbreak of COVID-19. We have to close our border and we saw the loss of income brought by tourism into the country.
Although our situation has declined in the past years but our status is still high in the OECD Developed Nation state. This status will not change for us because they can see the rise of tourism again as well as other businesses.
So, Mr Speaker, we have to look for ways to repay these loans that was not generated through tourism. We can see we have started our project on the Seabed Minerals amd you can see that there are two research vessels berthed at the wharf that had already started doing their work. We have to look to the near future to reap the benefits. But for the time being, we are relying on tourism.

I am now happy because it has been confirmed that three direct flights per week will be flying in from Australia to the Cook Islands. A direct flight from Hawaii will also commence. Last week, it was announced that there will be an increase of flights from Tahiti from one flight a week to three flights per week. So, our tourism industry is advancing.
Now on the question of inflation. The only way to beat inflation is to make money. As a country, that is what we need to do. Most of the inflation, if not all of the inflation is beyond our control. Even though the war in the Ukraine is on the other side of the world, we see the impact on the price of fuel and gas. Even though the impacts of climate change occur in other countries, we see that in the increase in the price of food.
Someone asked me why did the price of chicken went up so much. Here is the answer. These chickens are fed with wheat and there is a shortage of wheat and one of the wheat producing countries in the world is Ukraine and the shipment of wheat in the Ukraine were suspended, taken over by Russia. So, the price of feed went up and the price of chicken went up. We are mindful of these increase pressures on our people which is why I am very glad next month in May when we table our Budget before this House, this Budget will have measures that will address those inflations especially to the most vulnerable in our community.
We do need to support our elderly, those collecting the Pension, the children and the households on low incomes. I encourage the Members to take into account what we will table for our National Budget in May and urge the Members to support it strongly when it comes to the House for debate in the month of June. Thank you.
